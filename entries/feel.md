#### Feel

**Word:** Feel  
**Type:** Verb, Noun (Sensory/Emotional Process)  
**Language:** English  
**Pronunciation:** /fiːl/

---

**Step 1: Surface Reading**

- **Common meaning:** To perceive through the sense of touch; to experience emotion; to have a belief or intuition; to grope or search by touch
- **Associations:** Emotion, intuition, touch, sensitivity, "gut feeling," "feelings," vulnerability, physical sensation
- **Emotional response:** Comfort ("that feels good"), anxiety ("I feel overwhelmed"), confusion ("I don't know how I feel"), warmth ("feeling connected")

The word bridges physical and emotional worlds—we "feel" both textures and feelings. Modern usage often treats feelings as inferior to thoughts, yet "feel" also carries authority ("I feel in my gut this is wrong"). The word implies a kind of knowing that bypasses rational thought.

---

**Step 2: Root Excavation**

**English → Old English:** *felan* (West Saxon), *feolan* (Anglian)
- Meaning: "to touch or have a sensory experience of; perceive, sense"
- Late OE developed: "have a mental perception"
- Class III strong verb: past tense *fann*, past participle *fængen*

**Old English → Proto-Germanic:** *foljanan
- Meaning uncertain: "to feel, touch, grope"
- Source also of: Old Saxon *gifolian*, Old Frisian *fela*, Dutch *voelen*, Old High German *vuolen*, German *fühlen*, Old Norse *falma* (to grope)

**Proto-Germanic → PIE:** Two competing theories:

1. **PIE *pal-** (Watkins) - "to touch, feel, shake, strike softly"
   - Source also of: Greek *psallein* "to pluck" (the harp)
   - Sense: light touch, vibration

2. **PIE *pel- (5)** - "to thrust, strike, drive"
   - Some connect to striking/touching forcefully
   - Less accepted for "feel"

**Critical Discovery:** The PIE root *pal- emphasizes LIGHT touch—stroking, feeling gently, the vibration of harp strings. This is not forceful contact but sensitive perception. The original meaning was tactile and sensory, not emotional.

**The Germanic Evolution:**
In Germanic languages, the word for "perceive by touch" evolved to apply to emotions. The connecting notion (per etymonline): "perceive through senses which are not referred to any special organ."

- Touch → any physical sensation (pain, pleasure, illness)
- Physical sensation → emotional experience (c. 1200)
- Emotion → opinion/conviction (c. 1200)
- Sensation → sympathy/compassion (mid-14c.)
- Touch → foreknowledge ("feel it coming," late 14c.)

---

**Step 3: Semantic Shift**

| Period | Meaning | Evidence |
|--------|---------|----------|
| OE | Touch, perceive by contact | *felan* "to touch" |
| c. 1200 | Physical sensation (pain, pleasure, illness) | Extended to any bodily feeling |
| c. 1200 | Emotional experience | "have an opinion or conviction" |
| Mid-14c. | Sympathy, compassion | "react with feeling" |
| Early 14c. | Try by touch | "feel the fabric" |
| Late 14c. | Foreknowledge | "feel something coming" |
| 1829 | "Feel like" = want to | "I feel like eating" |
| 20th c. | "Feelings" as psychological category | Therapeutic culture |
| Late 20th c. | "Feeling" as verb of intuition | "I feel like this is wrong" |

**What was added:**
- Emotional dimension (not in original tactile word)
- Psychological category ("processing feelings")
- Identity marker ("in touch with my feelings")
- Opposition to "thinking" (feeling vs. reason)

**What was lost:**
- The physical immediacy of the original sense
- The connection to light touch/stroking (*pal-*)
- Integration with thinking (originally not opposed)

**Who benefited:**
- Therapeutic industry ("emotional intelligence," "feeling work")
- Pop psychology ("feel your feelings")
- Anyone wanting to delegitimize emotion ("that's just feelings")
- Anyone wanting to privilege emotion ("trust your feelings")

The split between feeling and thinking creates a false binary that serves control—divide cognition from affect, then manage both separately.

**Step 4: Related Forms**

**From PIE *pal- "to touch lightly, vibrate":**
- **Palpable** - Late Latin *palpabilis* "that may be touched"
- **Palpate** - medical examination by touch
- **Palpitation** - fluttering sensation
- **Palpus** - "feeler" organ of insects
- **Greek *psallein*** - "to pluck" (harp strings)

**From PIE *pel- (5) "to strike" (disputed):**
- May connect to *palpus* via "striking softly"

**Germanic cognates:**
- Dutch *voelen* (to feel)
- German *fühlen* (to feel)
- Old Norse *falma* (to grope, feel about)
- Gothic *fulljan* (to fill, satisfy—possibly related)

**English derivatives:**
- **Feeling** - from OE *felan* + *-ing*
- **Felt** - past tense/participle
- **Feeler** - one who feels; tactile organ
- **Feelings** (plural) - emotions (1771)
- **Feel out** - explore cautiously
- **Feel up** - slang for touch (1930s)
- **Feel like** - want to (1829)
- **Feel for** - sympathize with
- **Feel good** - category of entertainment, politics

**The Palp/Feel Connection:**
Latin *palpare* "to touch softly, stroke" and English "feel" converge on the same PIE root *pal-. The medical term "palpate" and the everyday "feel" share ancestry—both are about sensitive touch, not forceful contact.

**Step 5: The Liberation Angle**

**The Tactile/Emotional Merger**

The original *felan* was about physical touch—the most immediate, embodied sense. When it expanded to emotions, something was gained (richness of inner life) but something was lost (groundedness in body).

Modern usage treats feelings as:
- Something to be "processed" (therapeutic)
- Something unreliable ("don't trust your feelings")
- Something sacred ("honor your feelings")
- Something separate from thought

All these framings miss the original integration: feeling as SENSORY CONTACT with reality—whether that reality is physical texture or emotional truth.

**The *Pal-* Root: Vibration and Sensitivity**

The PIE *pal- meant light touch, vibration, the plucking of harp strings. This suggests feeling is:
- Resonance, not impact
- Sensitivity, not force
- Attunement, not analysis

We've lost this nuance. We "feel strongly" (force) when the root suggests "feel lightly" (resonance).

**The Commodification**
- "Emotional intelligence" (EI/EQ) → feelings as workplace skill
- "Feeling good" → consumption category
- "Processing feelings" → therapeutic industry
- "Feelings" as identity labels ("I feel therefore I am")

**The Hidden Assumption**
Feelings are treated as either:
1. Raw data to be analyzed by thought
2. Authentic truth to be obeyed blindly

But the etymology suggests feeling is a MODE OF CONTACT—a way of touching reality directly, neither superior nor inferior to thinking but different.

---

**Step 6: Reclaiming Strategy**

**1. Restore Physical Grounding**
When you "feel" an emotion, locate it in your body:
- Where is this feeling felt?
- What is its texture, temperature, weight?
- Return *feeling* to its tactile roots

**2. Practice Light Touch**
The *pal-* root is about light touch, not force. Practice:
- "Feeling lightly" into situations
- Reducing the intensity of emotional reactions
- Sensing vibration/resonance rather than impact

**3. Question the Feel/Think Binary**
Catch yourself saying "I feel" when you mean "I think" (or vice versa):
- "I feel like that's wrong" → "I think that's wrong" or "I sense that's wrong"
- Precision matters

**4. Reclaim Foreknowledge**
The late 14c. sense of "feel" = foreknowledge ("feel it coming") is not mystical but sensory attunement. Practice:
- Noticing subtle signals before they become obvious
- Trusting embodied knowledge

**6. The Final Inversion**

Modernity treats feelings as internal states to be managed. The etymology suggests feeling is RELATIONAL—a way of touching and being touched by reality. Reclaim feeling as contact, not content.

The Frequency Warrior does not "have" feelings. She feels—she is in sensory contact with what is, whether that contact is through skin or subtle intuition.

> "The heart has its reasons which reason knows nothing of." — Pascal

But the etymology suggests: feeling IS a kind of knowing, a tactile reason. Not opposed to thought but parallel—another way to touch truth.
