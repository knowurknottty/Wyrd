## Overview

## Entry Summaries

### 1. NORMAL (NOR-001)
**Root:** Latin *norma* (carpenter's square)  
**Key Revelation:** Normal was originally a tool, not a truth. The carpenter's square became the metaphorical standard for human behavior.  
**Liberation Angle:** Who holds the measuring tool? Normal is a measurement, not a moral fact.

### 2. ABNORMAL (ABN-001)
**Root:** Latin *ab-* (away) + *norma* (square)  
**Key Revelation:** Abnormality requires a norm-maker. Someone chose where to place the square.  
**Liberation Angle:** "Abnormal" is a position of perspective. From outside the center, the center is just another point.

### 3. STANDARD (STD-001)
**Root:** Frankish *standhard* (stand fast), military banner  
**Key Revelation:** The standard was originally a choice, not a command—a rallying point soldiers could choose to follow.  
**Liberation Angle:** Standards are banners; you can choose which ones to rally around.

### 4. DEVIANT (DEV-001)
**Root:** Latin *de via* (off the road)  
**Key Revelation:** All paths are built; all buildings serve builders. The via was constructed by empire.  
**Liberation Angle:** Deviation is not elevation but grounding—not off-course but on your own course.

### 5. MORAL (MOR-001)
**Root:** Latin *mos/mores* (custom, habit)  
**Key Revelation:** Moral was invented by Cicero to translate Greek *ethikos*. Customs became commands.  
**Liberation Angle:** Customs aren't commands. Return to character formation rather than rule-following.

### 6. IMMORAL (IMM-001)
**Root:** Latin *in-* (not) + *moralis* (moral)  
**Key Revelation:** The condemnation comes from the power behind "moral," not from negation itself.  
**Liberation Angle:** Reject the binary. Choose ethics over morality when possible.

### 7. ETHICAL (ETH-001)
**Root:** Greek *ethos* (character, custom)  
**Key Revelation:** Ethical originally meant character—not compliance. Aristotle: we become good by doing good.  
**Liberation Angle:** Return to character formation. Don't buy ethics; practice it.

### 8. CORRUPT (COR-001)
**Root:** Latin *com-* + *rumpere* (break completely)  
**Key Revelation:** Corruption is the breaking of something constructed—but who constructed it, and for whom?  
**Liberation Angle:** Not all breaking is corruption. Some breaking is liberation.

### 9. VALUE (VAL-001)
**Root:** Latin *valere* (to be strong, be worth)  
**Key Revelation:** Value is strength—but strength for whom? Value is fundamentally about power relations.  
**Liberation Angle:** Distinguish price from worth. Your value is not your market price.

### 10. WORTH (WOR-001)
**Root:** Old English *weorþ* (value, price, becoming)  
**Key Revelation:** Worth is relational turning, not intrinsic property. Connected to *weorþan* (to become).  
**Liberation Angle:** Worth is becoming, not possession. Your worth is your direction.

### 11. PRICE (PRI-001)
**Root:** Latin *pretium* (price, value, reward, prize)  
**Key Revelation:** Price is a story about sacrifice, not just a number. What you give up to obtain.  
**Liberation Angle:** Pluralize price: money price, time price, moral price. Not everything should have a price.

### 12. COST (CST-001)
**Root:** Latin *constare* (stand together, stand firm)  
**Key Revelation:** Cost is what stands between you and what you want—but standing things have their own reality.  
**Liberation Angle:** Cost is not just obstacle; it is information about what is real.

### 13. DEBT (DEB-001)
**Root:** Latin *debere* (to owe, to have from/away)  
**Key Revelation:** Debt is keeping what belongs to another—but who decides what belongs to whom?  
**Liberation Angle:** Not all debts should be paid. Some debts are manufactured; some should be forgiven.

### 14. CREDIT (CRE-001)
**Root:** Latin *credere* (to believe, trust), from PIE *kerd-dhe-* (put heart)  
**Key Revelation:** Credit is belief made economic—and belief comes from the heart.  
**Liberation Angle:** Don't let credit scores determine self-worth. Your value is not determined by algorithmic trust.

### 15. INTEREST (INT-001)
**Root:** Latin *interesse* (to be between)  
**Key Revelation:** Interest is being-between—but betweenness can be exploitation or engagement.  
**Liberation Angle:** Cultivate curiosity over extraction. Engage gaps; don't just profit from them.

### 16. PROFIT (PRF-001)
**Root:** Latin *proficere* (make forward, advance)  
**Key Revelation:** Profit is forward progress—but forward toward what? The narrowing of ends is the danger.  
**Liberation Angle:** Ask not just "how much?" but "toward what?" and "for whom?"

### 17. LOSS (LOS-001)
**Root:** Proto-Germanic *lausa-* (loose, dissolving), PIE *leu-* (loosen, cut)  
**Key Revelation:** Loss is loosening—but loosening can be liberation. Not all bonds should be maintained.  
**Liberation Angle:** Some losses are liberation—freedom from bonds that constrained.

### 18. RISK (RSK-001)
**Root:** Italian *risco* (danger, cliff, uncertain origin)  
**Key Revelation:** Risk was originally sailing close to cliffs—but who put the cliffs there?  
**Liberation Angle:** Question who bears the risk. Not all risks should be borne by the vulnerable.

### 19. SAFETY (SAF-001)
**Root:** Latin *salvus* (whole, uninjured), from PIE *sol-* (whole, well-kept)  
**Key Revelation:** Safety is wholeness—but wholeness requires boundaries, and boundaries can become prisons.  
**Liberation Angle:** Seek safety as wholeness, not stasis. Some growth requires leaving safety.

### 20. DANGER (DAN-001)
**Root:** Vulgar Latin *dominarium* (lord's power, jurisdiction)  
**Key Revelation:** Danger is the power of the lord. To be "in danger" was to be under someone's power.  
**Liberation Angle:** Danger is relational and political. The opposite of danger is sovereignty—self-rule.

## Thematic Connections

### Norms and Control
Words 1-4 (*normal, abnormal, standard, deviant*) reveal how social control operates through measurement and spatial metaphor. The carpenter's square (*norma*), the military banner (*standard*), the road (*via*)—all are tools of navigation that became tools of domination.

### Ethics and Judgment
Words 5-7 (*moral, immoral, ethical*) trace the shift from custom (*mos*) to command. Cicero's translation of Greek *ethikos* into Latin *moralis* began a long process of elevating habitual behavior to universal law.

### Economic Relationships
Words 9-17 (*value, worth, price, cost, debt, credit, interest, profit, loss*) form the vocabulary of exchange. Each reveals how economic concepts carry affective, relational, and even spiritual weight. The etymologies show economics was never separate from ethics, belief, and power.

### Risk and Protection
Words 18-20 (*risk, safety, danger*) complete the circle. Risk was sailing near cliffs; safety was wholeness (*salvus*); danger was lordship (*dominium*). Together they reveal that protection and threat are two aspects of the same power relations.
