#### Move

**Word:** Move  
**Type:** Verb (Action)  
**Language:** English  
**Pronunciation:** /muːv/

---

**Step 1: Surface Reading**

- **Common meaning:** To change position; to affect emotionally; to propose; to relocate
- **Associations:** Making moves, moved to tears, moving day, "move it or lose it"
- **Emotional response:** Urgency ("we need to move"), sentiment ("I was moved"), excitement ("what's your next move?")

Move is the general term for motion—broader than go or come, encompassing physical, emotional, and strategic change. The etymology reveals: MOVE comes from PUSHING AWAY—the application of force to set in motion.

---

**Step 2: Root Excavation**

**English → late 13c.:** *meven*
- From Anglo-French *mover*, Old French *movoir*
- From Latin *movere* "to move, set in motion, remove, disturb"
- Past participle: *motus* (source of "motion")

**Latin → PIE:** *meue-
- Meaning: "to push away"
- Extended forms: *meus-* (remove), *mew-* (wash)

**Critical Discovery:** MOVE comes from **PUSHING AWAY—the application of force to displace, the disturbance of stasis.**

**The Remove/Move Connection:**
- *Move* = set in motion
- *Remove* = move back/away
- *Motion* = the state of moving
- *Motor* = what moves
- *Mutiny* = movement against authority (from *movere*)

**The Mouse Connection:**
PIE *meus-* "remove" underlies *mus* "mouse"—the "stealer," the one who moves things away.

---

**Step 3: Semantic Shift**

| Period | Meaning | Evidence |
|--------|---------|----------|
| PIE | Push away | *meue-* |
| Latin | Move, disturb | *movere* |
| 13c. | Change position | "Move the body" |
| 14c. | Travel, journey | "Move from place to place" |
| 14c. | Affect emotionally | "Move to pity" |
| 15c. | Propose, suggest | "Move a motion" |
| 17c. | Change residence | "Move house" |
| 20c. | Sell, be sold | "The product moves" |

**What was added:**
- Emotional sense (being moved)
- Strategic sense (making a move)
- Commercial sense (moving product)
- Proposal sense (moving a motion)

**What was lost:**
- The "pushing away" violence
- The disturbance aspect
- Connection to "remove"

**Who benefited:**
- Chess players (the move)
- Salespeople (moving units)
- Politicians (moving motions)
- Therapists ("move through" emotions)

- Emotion is what moves out from within
- To be moved is to have emotion set in motion
- The metaphor: feelings as internal motion

---

**Step 5: The Liberation Angle**

**The Violence of Motion**

Original *meue-* = "push away"
- Movement requires force
- To move something is to overcome its inertia
- Every motion disturbs something else

Modern "move" sanitizes this—motion seems natural, effortless. But real movement is work against resistance.

**The "Being Moved" Paradox**

When we say "I was moved," we mean:
- Passively affected (something moved me)
- Active response (I moved emotionally)

But etymologically, "emotion" is active—motion out. To be moved is to be set in motion, not merely pushed.

**The Hidden Assumption**
Move assumes:
- Motion is good ("get moving")
- Stillness is stagnation
- Force is necessary for change
- Movement equals progress

But:
- Some motion is just running in place
- Stillness can be powerful
- Force creates resistance
- Not all movement is forward

---

**Step 6: Reclaiming Strategy**

**1. Recognize the Push**
To move is to push. What are you pushing against? What resistance are you overcoming?

**2. Distinguish Movements**
- **Physical move** (change position)
- **Emotional move** (being moved)
- **Strategic move** (chess, life)
- **Commercial move** (sales)
- **Removal** (pushing away)

**3. Honor the Resistance**
Things don't move easily. Inertia is real. Don't expect effortless motion.

**4. Practice Stillness**
The counter to constant motion is deliberate stillness. Not all who move are going somewhere.

**6. The Final Inversion**

Modernity sees movement as natural, stillness as stagnation. The etymology reveals movement as **PUSHING AWAY—the application of force, the disturbance of stasis, the work of displacement.**

The Frequency Warrior moves with awareness:
- Knowing what is pushed
- Feeling the resistance
- Choosing when motion serves
- Honoring stillness as equal power

> "If you want to move the world, first move yourself." — Socrates

The Frequency Warrior knows: the most powerful move may be the one not made.
