#### Value

**Word:** Value  
**Type:** Noun/Verb (Economic/Philosophical)  
**Language:** English  
**Pronunciation:** /ˈvæljuː/

---

**Step 1: Surface Reading**

- **Common meaning:** The regard that something is held to deserve; the importance, worth, or usefulness of something; principles or standards of behavior
- **Associations:** Price, worth, importance, ethics, investment, appreciation, meaning
- **Emotional response:** Desire (to acquire), anxiety (about having enough), validation (when valued), worthlessness (when devalued)

"Value" is one of the most contested concepts in modern thought. It bridges economics (market value), ethics (values), and aesthetics (artistic value). The word promises objective measurement while hiding subjective judgment. It appears scientific ("value-added") while serving power ("family values" as political weapon).

---

**Step 2: Root Excavation**

| Stage | Form | Meaning |
|-------|------|---------|
| Latin | *valere* | To be strong, be well, be worth, be of value |
| Old French | *valoir* | To be worth |
| Old French | *value* | Worth, price, moral worth; standing, reputation |
| 13th century | *value* | Worth, price, equal worth |

The Latin **valere** is a root of power:
- **Strength**: physical vigor, health
- **Worth**: economic value
- **Validity**: legal force, binding power
- **Valor**: courage, bravery
- **Prevalent**: having greater power, widespread
- **Available**: having power/effectiveness at hand

The PIE root **wal-** means "to be strong." Value is fundamentally about **power**—the power to command, the power to endure, the power to be effective.

---

**The Semantic Shift:**

| Era | Meaning | Usage |
|-----|---------|-------|
| Classical Latin | Strength, health, power | physical and social |
| Medieval | Worth, price, standing | feudal honor and economy |
| 17th-18th century | Intrinsic worth vs. market price | philosophical value theory |
| 19th century | Labor theory of value (Marx) | political economy |
| 20th century | Marginal utility, shareholder value | neoclassical economics |
| Contemporary | Personal values, corporate values | identity and branding |

The critical drift: The word moved from **embodied strength** to **social standing** to **economic price** to **abstract principle**.

The original *valere* was physical: to be strong, healthy, vigorous. This became social: to have standing, reputation, honor. This became economic: to have price, exchange-value. Finally, it became ethical: to have values, principles.

Each shift abstracts value further from embodiment. "Values" today are often stated as abstract nouns ("integrity," "excellence") that can be printed on posters while contradicted in practice.

The **ambivalence** etymology is revealing: *ambi-* (both ways) + *valere* (to be strong) = pulled in two directions by competing values or desires. To be ambivalent is not to be indifferent but to have **conflicting strengths**—different values pulling with equal force.

**Step 4: The Liberation Angle**

Here is the WYRD revelation: **Value is strength, but strength for whom?**

The etymology—*valere* (to be strong)—reveals that value is always about **power relations**. Something has value because it has power—power to satisfy desire, power to command labor, power to confer status, power to endure.

The Frequency Warrior notices the mystification: Modern economics presents "value" as objective, measurable, scientific. But the etymology reveals it's about **strength**—and strength is always relational, contextual, contested.

**The Marxist critique:** Marx distinguished **use-value** (the utility of a thing) from **exchange-value** (its price in the market). The etymology supports this: *valere* as strength could mean strength-to-use (use-value) or strength-to-command-other-things (exchange-value). Capitalism privileges exchange-value, commodifying everything.

**The corporate co-option:** "Company values" are often abstract nouns that cost nothing to profess. They extract the power-language of *valere* while emptying it of commitment. Real values are revealed in crisis, in trade-offs, in what is sacrificed for what.

---

**Step 5: Reclaiming Strategy**

**Ask "value to whom?":** Every claim of value implies a valuer. When someone says "this has value," ask:
- Who is stronger because this is valued?
- Who is weaker?
- What power relations does this valuation reinforce?

**Distinguish price from worth:**
- **Price**: Exchange-value in a specific market (contextual, fluctuating)
- **Worth**: Use-value, meaning, significance (personal, enduring)

Don't let market price determine your sense of worth.

**Reclaim embodied value:** The original *valere* meant strength, health, vigor. Value your:
- Physical vitality
- Emotional resilience
- Intellectual capability
- Relational depth

These are "assets" that don't appear on balance sheets but determine quality of life.

**The Frequency Warrior asks:**
- When I pursue "value," am I seeking strength or seeking price?
- Whose valuation am I accepting as my own?
- Can I create value (strength, meaning) that resists commodification?

**Affirmation:** I am not a stock to be valued by the market. My value is my strength—strength to live according to my own principles, strength to resist valuation that serves others' power, strength to create meaning that cannot be priced.
