#### Mind

**Word:** Mind  
**Type:** Noun, Verb (Concept)  
**Language:** English  
**Pronunciation:** /maɪnd/

---

**Step 1: Surface Reading**

- **Common meaning:** The element of a person that enables them to be aware, think, and feel; the faculty of consciousness and thought; intellectual capabilities
- **Associations:** Brain, intellect, thoughts, consciousness, sanity ("out of one's mind"), intention ("I have a mind to"), attention ("pay mind"), emotional state ("peace of mind")
- **Emotional response:** Reverent ("brilliant mind"), anxious ("losing my mind"), dismissive ("never mind"), protective ("my mind is my own"), or burdened ("weighing on my mind")

The word feels like a *place*—"in my mind," "the back of my mind," "frame of mind." It feels like a *thing* you can possess or lose. Modern usage treats mind as a container, a territory, a possession. Even the verb form "to mind" implies guarding or attending to something external.

---

**Step 2: Root Excavation**

**English → Old English:** *gemynd* (late 12th c., appearing as *mynd*)  
- Original meaning: "memory, remembrance; state of being remembered; thought, purpose; conscious mind, intellect, intention"
- The *ge-* prefix (perfective/collective) + *mynd* → that which holds or contains memories
- Old English also had *hyge* ("mind, spirit") for the thinking faculty—*gemynd* was specifically memory

**Old English → Proto-Germanic:** *ga-mundiz  
- Source also of: Gothic *muns* ("thought"), *munan* ("to think"); Old Norse *minni* ("mind"); German *Minne* (archaic "love," originally "memory, loving memory")
- The Germanic root emphasizes *remembrance* and *loving memory*

**Proto-Germanic → PIE:** *men- (1)  
- Meaning: "to think, remember, have one's mind aroused"—with derivatives referring to qualities and states of mind or thought
- The root carries the sense of *mental activity*, not a static thing

**Cognates across the PIE family:**
- **Sanskrit:** *manas-* ("mind, spirit"), *matih* ("thought"), *munih* ("sage, seer")
- **Avestan:** *manah-* ("mind, spirit")
- **Latin:** *mens* ("mind, understanding, reason"), *memini* ("I remember"), *mentio* ("remembrance")
- **Greek:** *memona* ("I yearn"), *mania* ("madness"), *mantis* ("one who divines, prophet, seer"), *ménos* ("mind, courage, anger")
- **Lithuanian:** *mintis* ("thought, idea")
- **Old Church Slavonic:** *mineti* ("to believe, think")
- **Russian:** *pamjat* ("memory")

**Critical Discovery:** The original meaning of *gemynd* was specifically **memory**, not thought in general. Old English used other words for the thinking faculty. This explains phrases like:
- *bear in mind* (late 14c.)
- *call to mind* (early 15c.)
- *keep in mind* (late 15c.)
- *time out of mind* (mid-14c., later legal term meaning "before Richard I" in 1189)
- *have mind of* (archaic: remember)

The "memory" sense survives in Scottish English and in these fossilized expressions.

**Step 3: Semantic Shift**

**Timeline of Transformation:**

| Period | Meaning | Evidence |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Late 12th c. | Memory, remembrance | *mynd* as "faculty of memory" |
| c. 1300 | Mental faculty, thinking process | Generalization begins |
| c. 1300 | Intention, purpose | "state of being minded to" |
| Late 14th c. | Frame of mind, mental disposition | Psychological state |
| Late 14th c. | Way of thinking, opinion | "in my mind" = opinion |
| Early 15th c. | Mind's eye (mental vision) | Remembrance → imagination |
| 1540s | "Have a mind" = be inclined | Intention as temporary state |
| 1784 | "Make up one's mind" | Decision as mental act |
| 1910 | "Pay no mind" (American dialect) | Attention as commodity |

**The Shift Explained:**

The word generalized from a *specific faculty* (memory) to encompass *all* mental faculties—thought, volition, feeling, and memory—gradually developing over the 14th and 15th centuries.

**What was added:**
- The container metaphor: "in my mind" (mind as place)
- The possession metaphor: "I have a mind" (mind as object)
- The territory metaphor: "frame of mind" (mind as bounded space)
- The mechanistic view: mind as processor, computer, machine

**What was lost:**
- The specific connection to *memory* and *remembrance*
- The active, process-oriented nature (*gemynd* as *activity* of remembering)
- The emotional/resonant quality (German *Minne* as "loving memory")
- The sense of mind as *relationship* to past experience

**Who benefited:**
The shift from "memory" (personal, embodied, relational) to "mind" (abstract, universal, mechanizable) paralleled the rise of:
- Cartesian dualism (mind/body split)
- Scientific materialism (mind as brain function)
- Psychological profiling (mind as measurable)
- Corporate "mindfulness" (mind as productivity tool)

A mechanistic, container-like mind is easier to manage, medicate, and market to.

**Step 4: Related Forms**

The PIE root *men- (1) "to think" spawned a vast family of words:

**Memory & Remembrance:**
- *Memory* — Latin *memoria* (from *memor* "mindful")
- *Remember* — "to call back to mind," from *re-* (again) + *memorari* "be mindful of"
- *Memento* — "remember!" (imperative of *meminisse* "to remember")
- *Memorial* — that which preserves memory
- *Mnemonic* — Greek *mnēmonikos* "of or pertaining to memory"
- *Mnemosyne* — Greek goddess of memory, mother of the Muses

**Mental Functions:**
- *Mental* — Latin *mentalis* "of the mind"
- *Mentation* — "mental function, the action or exercise of the mind" (1839)
- *Dementia* — Latin *de-* (away) + *mens* (mind)

**Communication & Warning:**
- *Mention* — Latin *mentio* "remembrance, calling to mind"
- *Admonish* — Latin *admonere* "to remind, suggest, warn"
- *Monitor* — Latin *monere* "to warn, advise"
- *Monition* — Latin *monitio* "warning, admonition"
- *Premonition* — "forewarning"
- *Summon* — Latin *summonere* "to remind privately," later "call to appear"

**Divination & Higher Knowing:**
- *Mania* — Greek *mania* "madness," from *mainesthai* "to rage, be furious"
- *Mantis* — Greek "one who divines, prophet, seer" (literally "thinker")
- *Mantra* — Sanskrit "instrument of thought," sacred utterance
- *Mentor* — from Mentor, advisor in the Odyssey, from Greek *menos* "mind, spirit"

**Money (unexpected cognate):**
- *Money* — Latin *moneta* (the mint, from the temple of Juno Moneta where coins were struck)
- Juno *Moneta* — "the Warner," she who admonishes/forewarns
- The word for money derives from the same root as "mind" and "memory"—perhaps because money *represents* value we must *remember*, or because the mint "reminds" us of state authority

**Other Notable Forms:**
- *Minerva* — Roman goddess of wisdom, from same root
- *Automatic* — Greek *automatos* "self-acting," from *autos* (self) + *men-* (think)
- *Museum* — Greek *mouseion* "seat of the Muses," from *men-* root via Mnemosyne
- *Music* — from the Muses, daughters of Memory
- *Monster* — Latin *monstrum* "divine omen, warning," from *monere* "to warn"
- *Monster* originally meant "that which warns"—a revealing of divine will

**Step 5: The Liberation Angle**

**Mind as Process vs. Mind as Thing**

The etymology reveals a profound metaphysical bait-and-switch. The original *gemynd* was not a *thing* but an *activity*—the activity of remembering, holding in awareness, being mindful. The PIE root *men- emphasizes *mental arousal*, *thinking*, *being mindful*.

But modern usage treats mind as:
- A **container** ("in my mind")
- A **possession** ("I have a mind")
- A **territory** ("frame of mind")
- A **machine** ("mental processing")

This reification—the turning of a process into a thing—has profound consequences:

1. **The Location Illusion:** "In my mind" suggests mind is a place. But where? The etymology suggests mind is not *in* anything—it is the *activity* of attending, remembering, thinking. You don't have thoughts *in* your mind; thinking *is* mind.

2. **The Ownership Trap:** "My mind" implies possession. But can you possess a process? You don't say "my running" or "my seeing" as if you own them. They are activities you *do*. What if mind is not something you *have* but something you *engage in*?

3. **The Objectification:** Treating mind as a thing makes it manageable, measurable, medical. It becomes the domain of experts who can tell you if your "mind" is working correctly. But if mind is relationship—*gemynd* as the relationship between present and past, self and world—then no expert can know it better than you.

4. **The Memory Connection:** The original meaning was *memory*. This suggests mind is fundamentally *relational*—it exists in relationship to what has been, what is held, what is remembered. A mind without memory is not a mind. This challenges the present-moment obsession of pop mindfulness: if mind is memory, then cultivating mind means cultivating *relationship with what has been*, not just presence.

**The Hidden Assumption:**
The word "mind" assumes a separation between the thinker and the thinking. It creates a subject ("I") who possesses or uses an object ("my mind"). But the etymology suggests mind *is* the activity, not its container.

**The Reversal:**
What if you don't *have* a mind? What if minding—attending, remembering, thinking—is what you *do*, and there is no separate thing called "mind" that does it? This dissolves the mind/body problem: mind is not a ghost in the machine; it is the *activity* of the organism, not separate from body but embodied activity itself.

**The Power Analysis:**
Reifying mind serves control. A "mind" can be:
- Diagnosed (psychiatry)
- Medicated (pharmaceutical industry)
- Optimized (productivity culture)
- Programmed (education systems)
- Marketed to (mindfulness industry)
- Weaponized (propaganda)

A *process* cannot be owned. A *thing* can.

**Step 6: Reclaiming Strategy**

**1. Notice the Container Metaphor**
When you say "in my mind," pause. What are you actually describing? A thought? A memory? An image? Be precise. Instead of "in my mind," try:
- "I am remembering..."
- "I am thinking..."
- "I am imagining..."
- "I am attending to..."

**2. Recover the Memory Connection**
The original *gemynd* was memory. Practice experiencing mind as *the activity of holding past in present*:
- When you "change your mind," notice: you are shifting what you hold in awareness
- When you "make up your mind," notice: you are committing to a pattern of attention
- When you "call to mind," notice: you are *literally* using the original meaning

**3. Question the Possession**
Catch yourself saying "my mind." Ask:
- Can I lose it? ("out of my mind")
- Can it be changed? ("change my mind")
- Can it be made up? ("make up my mind")

These phrases treat mind as object. Experiment with process language:
- Instead of "I changed my mind" → "I am thinking differently now"
- Instead of "make up your mind" → "commit to a direction"
- Instead of "out of your mind" → "not thinking clearly"

**4. Practice Minding (the Verb)**
The verb "to mind" survives in:
- "Mind the gap" (pay attention)
- "Do you mind?" (does this arouse your attention/annoyance?)
- "Never mind" (don't attend to it)

These preserve the original *activity* sense. Expand this usage:
- "I am minding my breath" (attending to)
- "What are you minding?" (what are you attending to?)
- "Mind how you go" (attend to your way)

**5. The Frequency Warrior's Glossary**

Replace "mind" with more precise terms:

| Instead of... | Try... |
|---------------|--------|
| "In my mind" | "I am thinking" / "I remember" / "I imagine" |
| "My mind is racing" | "I am thinking rapidly" |
| "Peace of mind" | "Peaceful attention" / "settled awareness" |
| "State of mind" | "How I am attending" |
| "Frame of mind" | "My perspective" / "my orientation" |
| "Brilliant mind" | "Clear thinker" / "subtle awareness" |
| "Losing my mind" | "Overwhelmed" / "confused" / "forgetting" |
| "Mind control" | "Attention manipulation" |
| "Mindfulness" | "Attending" / "remembering" |

**6. The Daily Practice**

Set a timer three times daily. When it rings, ask:
- "What am I minding right now?" (What has my attention?)
- "What am I holding in *gemynd*?" (What am I remembering?)
- "Is mind a place I am in, or an activity I am doing?"

**7. The Final Inversion**

The word "mind" has been turned from a verb (minding, attending, remembering) into a noun (the mind, a thing). This inversion is the pattern of control: turn activity into object, then the object can be owned.

Reclaim mind as **minding**.

You do not have a mind. You *mind*. You attend. You remember. You think. These are activities, not possessions. The moment you claim to "have" a mind, you have already accepted the frame that makes you separable from your own awareness.

The Frequency Warrior does not defend "her mind" from manipulation. She *minds* what matters, and leaves the rest unminded.

> "The mind is not a vessel to be filled but a fire to be kindled." — Plutarch

He was closer than he knew: mind is not a vessel at all. It is the kindling.
