#### Include

**Word:** Include
**Type:** Verb
**Language:** English
**Pronunciation:** /ɪnˈkluːd/ (British), /ɪnˈkluːd/ (American)

---

**Step 1: Surface Reading**

- **Common meaning:** To comprise or contain as part of a whole; to make part of, enclose within
- **Associations:** Inclusion, containing, adding, embracing, accommodating, welcoming
- **Emotional response:** Welcome, belonging, generosity, overwhelm, boundary concerns

---

**Step 2: Root Excavation**

**The Chain of Descent:**

| Stage | Form | Meaning | Notes |
|-------|------|---------|-------|
| **Modern English** | *include* | To contain as part of whole | From Latin via Middle English |
| **Middle English** | *inclosen* | To enclose, shut in | From Latin |
| **Latin** | *includere* | To shut in, enclose, imprison, insert | *in-* + *claudere* |
| **Latin** | *in-* | In, into | Directional prefix |
| **Latin** | *claudere* | To shut, close, enclose | From PIE *klau-* |
| **PIE** | *klau-* | Hook, peg, nail, pin | Things used as locks/bolts |

**The Critical Etymological Evidence:**

> "Latin *includere* 'to shut in, enclose, imprison, insert,' from *in-* 'in' + *claudere* 'to shut.'" — *Online Etymology Dictionary*

Latin *includere* means literally "to shut in" — *in-* (in) + *claudere* (to shut). The root *claudere* comes from PIE *klau-* meaning "hook, peg, nail" — the primitive hardware of locking. To include originally meant to enclose, confine, imprison — to put something inside and shut the door.

---

**Step 3: Semantic Shift**

**From Shutting In to Welcoming In**

| Era | Meaning | Context |
|-----|---------|---------|
| **PIE** | *klau-* — hook, peg | Physical locking hardware |
| **Latin** | *claudere* — to shut, close | Enclosure, confinement |
| **Latin** | *includere* — shut in, imprison | Often involuntary |
| **Medieval Latin** | *includere* — contain, comprise | Neutral inclusion |
| **Middle English** | *inclosen* — enclose, contain | Keeping in |
| **Modern English** | Include — make part of, welcome | Positive, voluntary |

**What Was Lost:**

The carceral dimension. Original *includere* often meant imprisonment — "shut in" as confinement. Modern "inclusion" is positive, welcoming, generous. The word has been completely inverted: from "keeping in" (prison) to "bringing in" (hospitality).

**What Was Added:**

Voluntary generosity. Modern inclusion implies choice — I choose to include you. The Latin suggests the opposite: once *includere* applied, you were shut in regardless of will. The power dynamic has shifted from imprisoner (forced inclusion) to host (chosen inclusion).

**The Enclose Connection:**

*Enclose* shares the root — both are about shutting in. But "enclose" retains more of the boundary sense (enclosed garden, enclosed space) while "include" has become purely positive. This semantic split allows "inclusion" to seem boundary-less.

**Step 5: The Liberation Angle**

**The Frequency Warrior's Hypothesis:**

> **Inclusion is shutting in — the question is whether it's prison or shelter. The etymology reveals enclosure, boundary, containment. Modern "inclusion" denies boundaries; ancient wisdom required them.**

Contemporary discourse celebrates "inclusion" as unconditional welcome. But *includere* means "shut in" — and shutting requires walls, doors, locks. You cannot include without excluding. The Frequency Warrior sees that healthy inclusion requires boundaries; boundary-less "inclusion" dissolves into formlessness.

**The Trap:**

Obligatory inclusion — the mandate to include everyone, everything, always. This creates overwhelm, boundary dissolution, and paradoxically: no one feels truly included because there's no "inside" to be in.

**The Reversal:**

Restore the gate. *Claudere* — to shut — is necessary for inclusion to mean anything. There must be an outside for there to be an inside. The gate swings both ways: inclusion for some requires exclusion of others. Choose your boundaries consciously.

**The Shift in Practice:**

| Boundless Inclusion | Bounded Inclusion |
|--------------------|-------------------|
| Everyone welcome | Some welcome, by choice |
| No outside | Clear inside/outside |
| Overwhelm | Security |
| Formlessness | Container |

---

**Step 6: Reclaiming Strategy**

Use this etymological knowledge to practice bounded inclusion:

**1. Acknowledge the gate**

Inclusion requires exclusion. You cannot include everyone. Choose who enters your space, your trust, your intimacy. This is not cruelty; it is clarity.

**2. Make inside worthwhile**

If *includere* is shutting in, make the enclosure worth entering. The prison becomes sanctuary when the inside is nurturing.

**3. Respect boundaries**

The same root gives *exclude*. Both are necessary. Don't shame others for their boundaries; honor your own.

**4. Practice enclosure**

Create physical, temporal, and social containers. Rituals enclose time. Homes enclose space. Communities enclose trust. These are forms of *includere*.

**The Frequency Warrior's Mantra:**

> *I shut the gate to open the circle. Inclusion requires enclosure; welcome requires walls. I choose who enters my sanctuary, and I make the inside worthy of the threshold. My boundaries are not cruelty; they are the shape of my care. I include with full awareness of the lock.*
