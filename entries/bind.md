#### Bind

**Word:** Bind  
**Type:** Verb (Action/Constraint)  
**Language:** English  
**Pronunciation:** /baɪnd/

---

**Step 1: Surface Reading**

- **Common meaning:** To tie or fasten with rope, cord, etc.; to restrain; to obligate; to cohere; to put a protective covering on
- **Associations:** Rope, ties, bonds, obligation, "binding contract," bookbinding, bandages, being stuck
- **Emotional response:** Constraint ("bound by duty"), security ("bound together"), anxiety ("I feel bound"), connection ("bound by love")

The word is dual: binding can imprison or protect, constrain or connect. Modern usage often emphasizes the negative (being trapped), but the etymology reveals binding is fundamentally about MAKING CONNECTION.

---

**Step 2: Root Excavation**

**English → Old English:** *bindan*
- Meaning: "to tie up with bonds" (literally and figuratively)
- Also: "to make captive; to cover with dressings and bandages"
- Class III strong verb: past tense *band*, past participle *bunden*

**Old English → Proto-Germanic:** *bindanan
- Source also of: Old Saxon *bindan*, Old Norse *binda*, Old High German *binten*, German *binden*, Gothic *bindan*

**Proto-Germanic → PIE:** *bhendh-
- Meaning: "to bind"

**PIE Root *bhendh- derivatives:**
- Sanskrit *badhnati* = "binds"
- Old Persian *bandaka-* = "subject"
- Lithuanian *bendras* = "partner"
- Middle Irish *bainna* = "bracelet"

**Critical Discovery:** The original meaning was **CONNECTION through binding**—not just constraint, but MAKING FAST, JOINING TOGETHER.**

**The Band/Bind/Bond Connection:**
All from same root:
- **Band** = flat strip, something that binds
- **Bind** = to tie
- **Bond** = that which binds (legal, chemical, social)
- **Bundle** = things bound together
- **Bend** = to curve (from bending a bow to string it)

---

**Step 3: Semantic Shift**

| Period | Meaning | Evidence |
|--------|---------|----------|
| PIE | Bind, make connection | *bhendh-* |
| Germanic | Tie up, fasten | *bindanan* |
| Old English | Bind literally and figuratively; bandage | *bindan* |
| c. 1400 | Bind books | Join pages |
| 1670s | Stick together, cohere | Intransitive sense |
| 1851 | "In a bind" = tight spot | Figurative constraint |
| Modern | Contractual obligation | "Binding agreement" |

**What was added:**
- Legal obligation sense
- Bookbinding specialty
- Metaphorical constraint ("in a bind")
- Negative associations (being trapped)

**What was lost:**
- The positive connection sense
- Bandaging/protective binding
- Partnership implications (*bendras*)
- The bend/bow connection

**Who benefited:**
- Legal systems (binding contracts)
- Publishers (bookbinding industry)
- Anyone wanting to emphasize constraint over connection

**Step 4: Related Forms**

**From PIE *bhendh- "to bind":**

**English cognates:**
- **Band** - flat strip, binding
- **Bandana** - from Hindi *bandhana* "to tie"
- **Bend** - curve (from bending bows)
- **Bond** - binding agreement
- **Bundle** - things bound together
- **Bund** - political association
- **Cummerbund** - waistband
- **Ribbon** - from *riban* ← *bhendh-*
- **Woodbine** - climbing plant that binds

**Other Germanic:**
- German *binden* (to bind), *Band* (band)
- Dutch *binden* (to bind)
- Gothic *bandi* "that which binds"

**Sanskrit:**
- *Badhnati* = "binds"
- *Bandhah* = "tying, bandage"

**The Bend/Bind Paradox:**
OE *bendan* = "to bend a bow" is causative of *bindan*!
- To bend a bow = to bind it with string
- Curving and tying are the same action
- This survives in "bent" (curved) and "bound" (tied)

---

**Step 5: The Liberation Angle**

**The Constraint/Connection Duality**

Binding is NEUTRAL:
- **Negative:** Prison, slavery, obligation
- **Positive:** Marriage, friendship, healing (bandage)
- **Neutral:** Bookbinding, bundling

The etymology emphasizes CONNECTION:
- *Bendras* (Lithuanian) = partner
- *Bainna* (Irish) = bracelet
- Bond = what holds together

Modern usage emphasizes CONSTRAINT. We've lost the positive sense.

**The Legal Capture**

"Binding contract" makes binding primarily legal:
- Obligation
- Enforcement
- Penalty for breaking

But the original was PHYSICAL and RELATIONAL:
- Tying things together
- Making fast
- Creating partnership

**The Bookbinding Metaphor**

Bookbinding is revealing:
- Takes separate pages
- Binds them into unity
- Creates something greater than parts

This is binding as INTEGRATION, not imprisonment.

**The Hidden Assumption**
Bind assumes:
- Constraint is primary
- Freedom = lack of binding
- Binding limits autonomy

But the etymology suggests:
- Connection is primary
- Freedom requires right binding
- We are bound or we are nothing (atoms, not molecules)

---

**Step 6: Reclaiming Strategy**

**1. Recognize Binding as Connection**
When you feel "bound," ask:
- Is this constraint or connection?
- Am I imprisoned or joined?
- What would happen if the binding dissolved?

**2. Value Positive Bonds**
Some bindings are good:
- Marriage bonds
- Friendship bonds
- Community bonds
- Bandages that heal

Don't throw away all binding in pursuit of abstract freedom.

**3. Practice Bookbinding Wisdom**
Integration requires binding:
- Ideas into philosophy
- People into community
- Moments into narrative

Binding makes meaning.

**4. Reject Only Constraint-Binding**
When binding is purely constraint (prison, slavery, forced obligation), resist. But don't confuse all binding with constraint.

**5. The Frequency Warrior's Glossary**

| Instead of... | Try... |
|---------------|--------|
| "I'm bound" | "I'm connected" / "I'm joined" |
| "Binding obligation" | "Fast commitment" |
| "In a bind" | "In a tight connection" |
| "Bound by duty" | "Connected through responsibility" |
| "Break free" | "Release from constraint" (not all bonds) |

**6. The Final Inversion**

Modernity sees binding as limitation. The etymology reveals binding as the MAKING of connection, relationship, integration.

The Frequency Warrior does not seek freedom FROM all binding. She seeks the RIGHT binding—connections that strengthen, obligations that dignify, bonds that liberate through belonging.

> "The only way to make sense out of change is to plunge into it, move with it, and join the dance." — Alan Watts

Binding is joining the dance—not being chained to the wall.
