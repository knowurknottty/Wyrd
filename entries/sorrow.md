#### Sorrow

**Word:** Sorrow  
**Type:** Noun, Verb (Emotion/State)  
**Language:** English  
**Pronunciation:** /ˈsɒroʊ/ (UK), /ˈsɔːroʊ/ (US)

---

**Step 1: Surface Reading**

- **Common meaning:** Deep distress, sadness, or regret; grief; mental suffering
- **Associations:** Grief, loss, mourning, melancholy, "sea of sorrow," heavy heart
- **Emotional response:** Heaviness, tears, empathy, catharsis, sometimes beauty ("sweet sorrow")

The word carries weight beyond mere sadness—sorrow as profound, lasting, dignified grief. Modern usage often distinguishes sorrow (earned through loss) from depression (pathological). The etymology reveals: SORROW comes from CARE/CONCERN—the Germanic root meant worry, care, sickness, not just sadness. Sorrow was being unwell in soul.

---

**Step 2: Root Excavation**

**English → Old English:** *sorg* / *sorh*
- Meaning: "sorrow, grief, care, anxiety, concern"
- Also "sickness, illness" in some cognates

**Old English → Proto-Germanic:** *sorgō
- Meaning: "care, worry, concern, anxiety"
- Cognates: Gothic *saurga*, Old Saxon *sorga*, Old High German *sorga*, German *Sorge*, Dutch *zorg*

**Proto-Germanic → PIE:** *suergh-
- Meaning: "to worry, be sick, be concerned"
- Extended form: *süergh-

**PIE Root:** *suergh-
- Meaning: "worry, care, illness"
- Cognates: Old Irish *serg* "illness," Lithuanian *sirgti* "to be ill"

**Critical Discovery:** SORROW comes from **CARE/WORRY/SICKNESS**—not just sadness but concern, anxiety, being unwell. The same root means "illness" in related languages. Sorrow was soul-sickness.

**The Germanic Sorge Family:**
Modern German *Sorge*:
- Means both "worry/concern" AND "sorrow/grief"
- Two senses preserved
- "Sorgen" = to worry
- "Sorge" = care, concern, sorrow

English "sorrow" lost:
- The "care" sense
- The "illness" connection
- Narrowed to grief only

**The Care/Worry Origin:**
*sorgō* = care, concern:
- Sorrow as caring deeply
- Grief shows what we value
- "Full of cares"
- Not just pain but investment

**The Illness Connection:**
Cognates in other languages:
- Old Irish *serg* = illness
- Lithuanian *sirgti* = to be ill
- *Suergh-* = sickness root

Sorrow as:
- Soul sickness
- Grief makes us ill
- Mind-body connection
- Ancient recognition

**Step 3: Semantic Shift**

| Period | Meaning | Evidence |
|--------|---------|----------|
| PIE | Worry, be sick | *suergh-* |
| Proto-Germanic | Care, concern, worry | *sorgō* |
| Gothic | Sorrow, care | *Saurga* |
| Old English | Sorrow, grief, care | *Sorg* |
| Old English | *Sorgful* = sorrowful | Adjective |
| Modern | Primarily grief/sadness | Narrowed |
| Modern German | Worry AND sorrow | *Sorge* (preserved) |

**What was added:**
- Deep grief sense emphasized
- Poetic/literary elevation
- "Sweet sorrow" (Shakespeare)
- Distinction from "sadness"

**What was lost:**
- "Care" sense (taken by "care")
- "Worry" sense (taken by "worry")
- "Illness" connection
- Active concern meaning

**Who benefited:**
- Literature (sorrow as theme)
- Religion ("man of sorrows")
- Psychology (distinguishing sorrow from depression)
- Music (sorrow as inspiration)

**The "Sorry" Question:**
Is "sorry" from "sorrow"?
- **Sorry** = from *sār* "sore, pained"
- Different root entirely!
- But converged in meaning
- "Sorry" = sore (pain), not sorrow (grief)

**The "Sore/Sorry" Root:**
- *Sār* = sore, pain, wound
- *Sārig* = painful, sore, later "sorry"
- Different from *sorg* = sorrow
- But both about pain
