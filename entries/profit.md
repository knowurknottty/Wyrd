#### Profit

**Word:** Profit  
**Type:** Noun/Verb (Economic)  
**Language:** English  
**Pronunciation:** /ˈprɒfɪt/ (UK) or /ˈprɑːfɪt/ (US)

---

**Step 1: Surface Reading**

- **Common meaning:** Financial gain; advantage or benefit; excess of returns over expenditure
- **Associations:** Business success, exploitation, greed, progress, self-interest, "profit motive"
- **Emotional response:** Aspiration (desire for), suspicion (of others'), justification ("for profit"), guilt (at profiting)

"Profit" is the central term of capitalism. It drives business decisions, measures success, and justifies risk. But "profit" also carries moral weight: "profiteering" is condemned, while "non-profit" signals virtue. The word reveals how economic calculation and moral judgment are inseparable.

---

**Step 2: Root Excavation**

| Stage | Form | Meaning |
|-------|------|---------|
| Latin | *proficere* | To make progress, be useful, succeed, profit |
| Latin | *pro-* | Forward |
| Latin | *facere* | To make, do |
| Latin | *profectus* | Advance, progress, increase, success |
| Old French | *profit* | Advantage, benefit, profit |
| 13th century | *profit* | Income, benefit, spiritual advantage |

The Latin **proficere** literally means **"to make forward"** or **"to do forward"** (*pro-* + *facere*). The past participle **profectus** meant advance, progress, growth, increase.

The PIE roots:
- **per-** (forward) gives us **pro-**, **for-**, **fur-**
- **dhe-** (to set, put, do) gives us **do**, **deed**, **facere**

Profit is fundamentally **forward movement**—making progress, advancing, growing.

---

**The Semantic Shift:**

| Era | Meaning | Usage |
|-----|---------|-------|
| Classical Latin | To advance, make progress, succeed | general achievement |
| Late Latin | To be useful, do good, benefit | moral/ethical |
| Medieval | Income, spiritual benefit, advantage | feudal economy |
| 16th-17th century | Financial gain from capital | mercantile capitalism |
| 18th-19th century | Surplus value, return on investment | classical economics |
| 20th-21st century | Shareholder value, profit maximization | neoliberalism |

The critical drift: The word moved from **general advancement** to **specific financial gain**.

In Latin, *proficere* described any kind of progress or success. One could *proficere* in learning, in virtue, in a journey. But modern "profit" is almost exclusively financial—the excess of revenue over costs.

The narrowing reflects capitalism's colonization of language: what was once general progress became specific monetary gain.

**Step 3: Related Forms**

| Word | Origin | Meaning |
|------|--------|---------|
| **Proficient** | Latin *proficiens* | Making progress, skilled, accomplished |
| **Profitability** | Latin-derived | Quality of being profitable |
| **Profiteer** | English formation | One who makes excessive profits (negative) |
| **Non-profit** | Modern | Organization without profit motive |
| **Pro-** | Latin *pro* | Forward, for (prefix) |
| **Fact** | Latin *factum* | Thing done (from *facere*) |
| **Factory** | Latin *factorium* | Place where things are made |

The **proficient/profit** connection is beautiful:
- **Proficient**: One who makes progress, who advances in skill
- **Profit**: Originally, any kind of advancement

Both are about **forward movement**. But we've separated them: "proficiency" is personal development; "profit" is financial extraction. The etymology reminds us they were once the same.

---

**Step 4: The Liberation Angle**

Here is the WYRD revelation: **Profit is forward progress—but forward toward what?**

The etymology—*pro-facere* (make forward)—reveals that profit is fundamentally **directional**. It asks: Are we making progress? But progress toward what end? And for whom?

The Frequency Warrior notices the assumed telos: In capitalism, "profit" assumes forward movement (growth) is always good. But the etymology doesn't specify the destination. "Profit" in Latin could be progress in virtue, in learning, in community—not just in money.

**The narrowing of ends:** Modern profit is end-less in two senses:
1. It has no *end* (goal) beyond itself—profit for more profit
2. It treats others as *means* without *ends* of their own

This is the opposite of the original *proficere*, which was always progress *toward* something—usefulness, benefit, success in a defined endeavor.

**The profiteering accusation:** When profit becomes excessive, we call it "profiteering." This reveals that profit carries moral weight. We accept profit as "progress" but condemn it when it's "too much." The boundary reveals social norms about legitimate gain.

---

**Step 5: Reclaiming Strategy**

**Broaden the ends:** Don't let profit mean only financial gain. Ask:
- What *pro-ficere* (forward-making) am I pursuing?
- Is this progress toward genuine benefit or mere accumulation?
- Who profits, and what kind of profit?

**Distinguish profit from progress:**
- **Financial profit**: Revenue minus costs (accounting)
- **Human profit**: Benefit, advancement, flourishing (ethics)
- **Ecological profit**: Regeneration, sustainability (systems)

Not all profit is monetary. Not all monetary profit is beneficial.

**Resist profit-maximization:** The idea that profit should be "maximized" is historically recent. The original *proficere* was about *making progress*, not *maximizing extraction*. Enough is different from maximum.

**The Frequency Warrior asks:**
- When I seek profit, what forward movement do I want to make?
- Can I profit (advance) without others losing (retreating)?
- What would *proficere* toward collective flourishing look like?

**Affirmation:** I pursue profit as progress—forward movement toward genuine benefit. I reject profit as mere extraction, accumulation without end. I ask not just "how much?" but "toward what?" and "for whom?"
