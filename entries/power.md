#### Power

**Word:** Power  
**Type:** Noun (Concept)  
**Language:** English  
**Pronunciation:** /ˈpaʊər/

---

**Step 1: Surface Reading**

- **Common meaning:** The capacity to influence, control, or coerce others; authority; strength; energy
- **Associations:** Control, dominance, hierarchy, electricity, force, corruption, empowerment
- **Emotional response:** Fear (of being controlled), desire (to possess it), awe, resentment, ambition

In modern usage, "power" carries heavy baggage. It suggests someone *over* someone else. We speak of "power struggles," "power grabs," and "power corrupts." The word feels metallic, hard, unyielding—something wielded rather than shared.

---

**Step 2: Root Excavation**

| Stage | Form | Meaning |
|-------|------|---------|
| Middle English | *pouer* / *poeir* | ability to act, strength |
| Anglo-Norman French | *poeir* / *poer* | to be able |
| Old French | *povoir* | ability, power |
| Vulgar Latin | *potere* | to be able |
| Classical Latin | *posse* | to be able, have power |
| Latin | *potis esse* | to be powerful/master |
| Latin | *potis* | powerful, able, capable |
| Proto-Italic | *potis* | master, husband |
| PIE | **\*poti-** | powerful; lord; master of house |

The journey is remarkable: **PIE \*poti-** (lord/master) → **Latin *potis*** (powerful, capable) combines with ***esse*** (to be) → ***posse*** (to be able) → evolves into Vulgar Latin ***potere*** → Old French ***povoir*** → Anglo-Norman ***poeir*** → Middle English ***power***.

**Sources:**
- Online Etymology Dictionary: *posse* "have power, be able," contraction of *potis esse* "be powerful, be master"
- Oxford English Dictionary: From Anglo-Norman French *poeir*, alteration of Latin *posse* "be able"
- Wedgwood's *Dictionary of English Etymology* (1872): Via Old French *povoir* from Latin *potere*

---

**The Semantic Shift:**

| Era | Meaning | Usage |
|-----|---------|-------|
| Original | Being capable/able | "I have the power to lift this" |
| Medieval | Military might | "the power of the king" |
| Early Modern | Political authority | "the powers that be" |
| Industrial | Mechanical/electrical energy | "power plant" |
| Contemporary | Domination/control | "power dynamics," "power over" |

The critical drift: The word moved from **capacity** (internal ability) to **domination** (external control). Originally, power described what one *could* do. Now it describes what one *makes others do*.

**Lost in translation:** The original Latin *potis* meant "capable" and "preferable"—suggesting excellence through ability, not coercion. *Posse* was simply "to be able." The violent connotations came later.

**The cognate web reveals the original meaning:** *Possible* and *possibility* preserve the old sense of "ability" better than *power* itself. When we say "it's possible," we invoke the ancient root without the modern baggage of domination.

**Step 4: The Liberation Angle**

Here is the WYRD revelation: **Power originally meant capacity, not control.**

The Latin *posse* meant simply "to be able"—the root of *possible*. When someone says "I don't have the power," they often mean "I don't have authority over others." But in the original sense, they may have *tremendous* power—the power to learn, to create, to heal, to connect.

The Frequency Warrior notices the bait-and-switch: We've been taught that power = domination. This serves those who hoard authority. If power means "control over others," then most people feel powerless. But if power means "capacity to act"—the original meaning—then power is abundant, democratic, renewable.

**The two faces of power:**
1. **Power as capacity** (*potentia*): What you *can* do—create, heal, understand, build
2. **Power as domination** (*potestas*): What you *make others do*—command, coerce, control

Modern language has collapsed these into one word, privileging the domination meaning. This linguistic conquest makes power seem scarce, zero-sum, violent.

---

**Step 5: Reclaiming Strategy**

**Mental substitution:** When you encounter "power," mentally substitute "capacity." Ask: "What capacity is actually being discussed?"

- "He has power over the department" → "He has *control* over the department" (reveals the domination frame)
- "I feel powerless" → "I feel *unable*" → "What capacities do I actually possess?"
- "Knowledge is power" → "Knowledge is *capacity*" (closer to the original meaning)

**Linguistic resistance:** Use *capacity*, *ability*, *potential*, or *capability* when you mean the constructive sense. Reserve *power* for domination contexts—this exposes the mechanism.

**Affirmation of latent power:** You contain *potentia*—untapped capacity. The word "potential" shares the root. You are not powerless; you are *power-full* in the original sense—able, capable, possible.

**The Frequency Warrior asks:**
- When I say "I want power," do I mean capacity or control?
- When I feel "powerless," what capacities am I overlooking?
- Can I reclaim *posse*—the simple ability to be and do?
