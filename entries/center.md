#### Center

**Type:** Noun/Verb (Position)  
**Language:** English

**The Etymological Evidence:**

> "late 14c., 'middle point of a circle; point round which something revolves,' from Old French centre (14c.), from Latin centrum 'center,' originally the fixed point of the two points of a drafting compass (hence 'the center of a circle'), from Greek kentron 'sharp point, goad, sting of a wasp,' from kentein 'stitch,' from PIE root *kent- 'to prick.'" — *Online Etymology Dictionary*

*Center* derives from *sharp point* — the *kentron* was a goad for cattle, the sting of a wasp, the sharp point that fixes and directs.

The semantic path: sharp point → goad → fixed point of compass → center of circle → middle of anything → focus of activity.

**Critical insight:** Center is originally *sharp point* — the penetrating, fixing, directing point. The center is not merely "middle" but *point of fixation* — what draws attention and activity to itself.

**What Was Lost:**

The original *kentron* sense of "sharp point, goad" has been completely abstracted. The center is no longer felt as *point* — the penetrating, potentially painful fixation. We've domesticated the center into comfortable "middle."

The goad (*kentron*) directs cattle by pain/pressure. The wasp sting (*kentron*) fixes attention through sharp sensation. The center commands by being point — the place where energy converges, potentially piercing.
