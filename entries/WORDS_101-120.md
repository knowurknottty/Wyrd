## The Entries

### 101. DAY
**Theme:** Daily Cycles  
**Root:** PIE *dhegh-* "to burn" or *agh- "a day" → Proto-Germanic *dagaz → OE *dæg*  
**Key Discovery:** Day originally meant "the burning/heating time"—day as heat, not just light. Day was fire-time, contrasting with cool night.

**Semantic Core:** Day comes from HEAT. The PIE root *dhegh-* "burn" also gives "fever"—day was the fever-time of activity. Old English reckoned days from sunset to sunset, so "Monday night" was what we call Sunday night.

**Reclamation:** Remember day as fire—as heat that warms but can also exhaust. Don't demand constant burning; fire needs fuel.

---

### 102. NIGHT
**Theme:** Daily Cycles  
**Root:** PIE *nekwt- → Proto-Germanic *nahts → OE *niht*  
**Key Discovery:** Night was the PRIMARY time-reckoning unit for Indo-Europeans. They "reckoned by nights"—a journey took X nights, not days.

**Semantic Core:** Night from PIE *nekwt-, possibly from *neg- "to be dark." The Aryans had a common word for night but not day because they measured by completed nights. Gothic *nahts* = night; *rasta* (mile) = distance between rest stops.

**Reclamation:** Count by nights survived, not days produced. The night is when integration happens; darkness is healing, not threatening.

---

### 103. MORNING
**Theme:** Daily Cycles  
**Root:** PIE *merk-/*mergʰ- "blink, twinkle" → PGmc *murgana- → OE *morgen*  
**Key Discovery:** Morning comes from TWINKLING—the uncertain flickering light before full dawn. The "glimmer of morning twilight."

**Semantic Core:** Morning was the twilight zone, not yet day. *Morgen* also meant "tomorrow"—the day began with morning. The "morgengifu" was the morning-gift given after wedding night.

**Reclamation:** Morning is liminal—neither night nor day. Don't demand full productivity; let the light flicker and grow.

---

### 104. EVENING
**Theme:** Daily Cycles  
**Root:** Proto-Germanic *ēbanþs (uncertain beyond) → OE *ǣfen* → *ǣfnung* (verbal noun)  
**Key Discovery:** Evening was originally a VERBAL NOUN—the "even-ing," the process of becoming evening, not a static time.

**Semantic Core:** *Ǣfnung* = "the coming of evening, sunset." From *ǣfnian* "to become evening." The word named the process, not the period. "Eve" (c. 1200) was evening, later narrowed to specific pre-event evenings (Christmas Eve).

**Reclamation:** Evening is becoming, not being. It evenings—let it happen gradually, don't rush to night.

---

### 105. DAWN
**Theme:** Daily Cycles  
**Root:** OE *dagian* "to become day" → PGmc *dagāną → PIE *dhegh-*  
**Key Discovery:** Dawn is simply "TO DAY"—the verb form of day. Modern "dawn" (noun) is a back-formation from "dawning."

**Semantic Core:** *Dagian* = "to become day, to dawn." The noun came FROM the verb. German still uses "tagen" as verb: "Es tagt" = "It dawns." "It dawned on me" preserves this verbal, gradual quality.

**Reclamation:** Dawn is not a thing but a process. The day dawns; we don't just "have dawn." Understanding dawns gradually too.

---

### 106. DUSK
**Theme:** Daily Cycles  
**Root:** OE *dox* "dark-colored" → possible PIE *dheus-* "dust, vapor"  
**Key Discovery:** Dusk was originally an ADJECTIVE meaning "dark, dusty, swarthy" before becoming a time word. The "dusty time."

**Semantic Core:** *Dox* meant "dark-colored" (of skin, objects), not time. Related possibly to *dheus-* "dust"—dusk is when dust motes become visible in slanted light. Related to Latin *fuscus* "dark, dusky."

**Reclamation:** Dusk is quality, not clock-time. It's the dust-colored time, when atmosphere becomes visible. Let it be undefined, liminal.

---

### 107. SLEEP
**Theme:** Rest and Consciousness  
**Root:** PIE *sleb-/*sleg- "be weak, slack" → PGmc *slēpaz → OE *slǣp*  
**Key Discovery:** Sleep comes from WEAKNESS/SLACKNESS—loosening of tension. Sleep and "slack" share a root. Old English had a DIFFERENT word for sleep: *swefn* (related to Greek *hypnos*).

**Semantic Core:** *Sleb-* = "weak, slack." Sleep is slackening of consciousness, loosening of muscular and mental tension. OE had *slǣp* (our word) AND *swefn* (PIE *swep-* like Greek *hypnos*). We kept the Germanic word, lost the Indo-European one.

**Reclamation:** Sleep is not "active rest" but slackening. Let go, don't control. Sleep and death were always kin—"the big sleep" is ancient.

### 108. DREAM
**Theme:** Rest and Consciousness  
**Root:** TWO SEPARATE WORDS:  
1. OE *drēam* "joy, music, merriment" (native)  
2. PGmc *draumaz* "dream vision" (Scandinavian influence) → PIE *dʰrewgʰ-* "deceive"  
**Key Discovery:** TWO COMPLETELY DIFFERENT WORDS merged! *Drēam* meant "joy, music" in Old English—the sleep-vision word came from Norse. Dreams are both JOY and DECEPTION.

**Semantic Core:** Old English *drēam* = joy, music, noisy merriment (NOT sleep vision). OE used *swefn* for sleep-dreams. Norse *draumr* invaded and merged. PIE *dʰrewgʰ-* = "deceive, harm"—related to Old Norse *draugr* "ghost," German *Trug* "deception."

**Reclamation:** Dreams are music AND deception. Enjoy without believing. The *drēam* (joy) may be more true than the *draumr* (vision).

---

### 109. WAKE
**Theme:** Rest and Consciousness  
**Root:** PIE *weg-* "be strong, lively" → PGmc *wakēną → OE *wacan*/*wacian*/*wæccan*  
**Key Discovery:** Wake comes from VIGOR/STRENGTH—not just "not sleeping" but "being lively." Same root as "vigor," "watch," "vegetable" (growing = lively).

**Semantic Core:** *Weg-* = "be strong, lively." Old English had THREE verbs: *wacan* (happen to wake), *wacian* (be awake), *wæccan* (cause to wake → "watch"). The funeral "wake" is vigil—staying awake as strength/sacrifice.

**Reclamation:** Waking is vigor, not just consciousness. Wake vigorously. The vigil tradition—staying awake for grief—is staying strong through the night.

---

### 110. REST
**Theme:** Rest and Consciousness  
**Root:** PGmc *rasto (uncertain PIE) → OE *ræst*  
**Key Discovery:** Rest meant STOPPING PLACE ON A JOURNEY—originally a spatial term. Gothic *rasta* = "mile" (distance between rest stops)!

**Semantic Core:** *Rasto* = resting place, stage of a journey. Gothic *rasta* meant "mile"—the measure was rest-distances! Different from Latin *restare* "remainder"—we merged two words. "The rest" (remainder) + "rest" (repose) = convergence.

**Reclamation:** Rest is a station, not destination. Journey continues. Also: "the rest" (what remains) comes from same root—rest contains what remains.

---

### 111. LABOR
**Theme:** Work and Effort  
**Root:** Latin *labor* (uncertain origin, possibly from *labere* "totter")  
**Key Discovery:** LABOR's origin is uncertain but clearly meant TOTTERING UNDER BURDEN—work as struggle, pain, hardship. "In labor" (childbirth) preserves the original agony.

**Semantic Core:** Latin *labor* = "toil, exertion; hardship, pain, fatigue." Possibly from *labere* "to totter, sink"—tottering under burden. Native English word was *weorc* (work). *Labor* imported the sense of suffering.

**Reclamation:** Labor hurts, and that's honest. "Labor of love" acknowledges both. The pain is part of it. Don't sanitize work as neutral "productivity."

---

### 112. TOIL
**Theme:** Work and Effort  
**Root:** TWO SEPARATE WORDS:  
1. *Toil* (work) = Latin *tudiculare* "crush with hammer" → PIE *(s)teu-* "strike"  
2. *Toil* (net) = Latin *tela* "web" → PIE *teks-* "weave"  
**Key Discovery:** TWO UNRELATED WORDS with same spelling! Toil (work) from CRUSHING OLIVES. Toil (net) from WEAVING. "Caught in the toils" preserves the net sense.

**Semantic Core:** *Toil* (work) = from *tudicula* "mill for crushing olives"—repetitive striking labor. *Toil* (net) = from *tela* "web, warp"—related to "textile," "toilet" (small cloth). Work-toil and net-toil converged: work is a snare.

**Reclamation:** Work can crush like hammer on olive. Work can trap like net. Both toils warn: labor can be bondage.

---

### 113. EFFORT
**Theme:** Work and Effort  
**Root:** Latin *ex-* "out" + *fortis* "strong" → Vulgar Latin *exfortiare* → OFr *esforcier*  
**Key Discovery:** EFFORT literally means "SHOWING FORTH STRENGTH"—*ex-* (out) + *fortis* (strong). Same family as "fort," "force," "comfort."

**Semantic Core:** *Exfortiare* = "to show strength, put forth strength." Effort makes strength visible, external. The "effortless" ideal (1752) emerged—grace without showing strain.

**Reclamation:** Effort is display, not just being. Showing strength is natural. But effortless grace is also mastery—know when to exert and when to flow.

---

### 114. EASE
**Theme:** Work and Effort  
**Root:** Latin *adiacēre* "lie near" → *adiacens* → OFr *aise*  
**Key Discovery:** EASE comes from LYING NEAR—proximity to comfort/opportunity. Originally "opportunity" as well as "comfort."

**Semantic Core:** *Ad-* (near) + *iacēre* (lie) = "lying near, adjacent." *Aise* meant both comfort AND opportunity. "At ease" = situated comfortably. Related to "adjacent" (same root, kept meaning).

**Reclamation:** Ease is adjacency, not possession. Be near comfort. Also: ease = opportunity—"at ease" means positioned for possibility.

---

### 115. COMFORT
**Theme:** Work and Effort  
**Root:** Latin *con-* (intensive) + *fortis* "strong" → Late Latin *confortare*  
**Key Discovery:** COMFORT originally meant STRENGTHEN GREATLY—not "make soft" but "make strong." Same root as "fort," "force," "effort."

**Semantic Core:** *Confortare* = "strengthen much, invigorate, give courage." Used in Vulgate Bible. Shifted from strengthening to soothing. "Comfort zone" should be a FORT, not a prison.

**Reclamation:** True comfort builds strength. Comfort is not the opposite of growth; it fortifies for challenge. *Confortare* = become strong, then venture.

---

### 116. PAIN
**Theme:** Emotions and States  
**Root:** PIE *kwei- "pay, atone" → Greek *poinē* → Latin *poena* → OFr *peine*  
**Key Discovery:** PAIN originally meant PUNISHMENT/PENALTY—not physical sensation. "On pain of death" = "on penalty of death" (preserves original).

**Semantic Core:** *kwei-* = "pay, atone, compensate." *Poinē* = blood money, penalty. *Poena* = punishment (Roman spirit of punishment). Physical pain sense came later. Same root: penal, penalty, punish, penance, subpoena.

**Reclamation:** Pain is not necessarily punishment. But the compensation root (*kwei-*) reminds us: pain signals imbalance, demands response. Medical, not moral—but not meaningless.

---

### 117. PLEASURE
**Theme:** Emotions and States  
**Root:** PIE *pl(e)hk-* "agree, be pleasant" → Latin *placere* "be acceptable" → OFr *plaisir*  
**Key Discovery:** PLEASURE comes from being PLEASED/AGREEABLE—*placere* meant "be acceptable, approved." Pleasure is fundamentally relational.

**Semantic Core:** *Placere* = "be acceptable, be liked, be approved." Related to *placare* "soothe, placate." Early "please" meant "satisfy a deity"—propitiate. Pleasure requires someone pleased.

**Reclamation:** Pleasure is relational, not private. "My pleasure" acknowledges connection. *Placere/placare*: pleasure and peace are one. Seek harmony, not just sensation.

---

### 118. JOY
**Theme:** Emotions and States  
**Root:** PIE *gau- "rejoice" → Latin *gaudere*/*gaudium* → OFr *joie*  
**Key Discovery:** JOY comes from REJOICING—the verb *gaudere*. Joy was expression, not just feeling. Latin had *gaudia* (expressions, plural) and *gaudium* (feeling, singular).

**Semantic Core:** *Gaudere* = "to rejoice." *Gaudia* (plural) = expressions of pleasure. *Gaudium* (singular) = the feeling. English "joy" collapsed both. "Enjoy" = "in" + *joie* = "give joy to" (originally transitive: "it enjoys me").

**Reclamation:** Joy must be expressed. *Gaudia* = expressions. Rejoice actively; joy unexpressed is incomplete. Joy is not possessed but performed.

---

### 119. SORROW
**Theme:** Emotions and States  
**Root:** PIE *suergh- "worry, be sick" → PGmc *sorgō → OE *sorg*  
**Key Discovery:** SORROW comes from CARE/WORRY/SICKNESS—not just sadness. Cognates mean "illness" in other languages. German *Sorge* still means both "worry" and "sorrow."

**Semantic Core:** *Sorgō* = care, concern, worry, anxiety. Also meant illness in cognates (Old Irish *serg*, Lithuanian *sirgti*). Sorrow was soul-sickness. You only sorrow for what you cared about.

**Reclamation:** Sorrow measures love. *Sorgō* = care—grief shows investment. German *Sorge* preserves this: to care is to risk sorrow. They are one.

---

### 120. HOPE
**Theme:** Emotions and States  
**Root:** PIE *kēw-/*keh₂u- "expect, wait" → PGmc *hupōną → OE *hopa*  
**Key Discovery:** HOPE originally meant EXPECTATION/WAITING—not necessarily positive. "I hope" meant "I expect," not "I wish optimistically."

**Semantic Core:** *Hupōną* = to expect, look forward to, wait. Hope was anticipation without guaranteed positive outcome. Related possibly to *keu-* "see, observe"—watching for what comes.

**Reclamation:** Hope is not blind optimism. It is readiness, preparation. *Hupōną* = waiting with attention. Hope plus action = power. "We'll see" as hope.

## Thematic Patterns

### Daily Cycles (101-106)
All six time-words reveal process over stasis:
- **Day** = burning/becoming
- **Night** = the measure of time
- **Morning** = twinkling/uncertain light
- **Evening** = the process of darkening
- **Dawn** = the verb "to day"
- **Dusk** = quality of light, not clock-time

### Rest and Consciousness (107-110)
All four involve slackening and strength paradox:
- **Sleep** = slackness, weakness (necessary)
- **Dream** = joy AND deception (two merged words)
- **Wake** = vigor, strength, liveliness
- **Rest** = stopping place on journey (not destination)

### Work and Effort (111-116)
All six reveal struggle, showing forth, and fortification:
- **Labor** = tottering under burden, pain
- **Toil** = crushing OR entrapment (two merged words)
- **Effort** = showing strength outward
- **Ease** = lying near (not achievement)
- **Comfort** = strengthening greatly (not softening)

### Emotions and States (117-120)
All four are fundamentally relational:
- **Pain** = penalty/compensation (social)
- **Pleasure** = being agreeable/approved (relational)
- **Joy** = rejoicing/expressing (active)
- **Sorrow** = care/concern (attachment)
- **Hope** = waiting/expecting (future-oriented)

## Key Linguistic Discoveries

### Merged Words (Homonyms)
Three entries involve two unrelated words that merged:
1. **Dream** (joy + vision)
2. **Toil** (crushing + weaving)
3. **Rest** (stopping + remainder)

### Lost Verbs
Several words were originally verbs:
1. **Dawn** = "to day" (verb form)
2. **Evening** = "the even-ing" (process)
3. **Joy** = from *gaudere* (to rejoice)

### PIE Roots with Surprising Connections
- *dhegh-* "burn" = day, fever, heat
- *sleb-* "slack" = sleep, slack
- *kwei-* "pay" = pain, penal, penalty, punish
- *pl(e)hk-* "agree" = please, placate, pleasure
- *suergh-* "worry" = sorrow, sick, care

## Conclusion

This batch traverses the fundamental rhythm of human existence: the cycles of light and dark, the oscillation between consciousness and rest, the necessity of work, and the spectrum of feeling from pain to joy. Each word reveals that our ancestors understood these experiences not as abstract categories but as processes, relationships, and bodily realities.

The Frequency Warrior's glossary from each entry offers alternatives to modern usage that often flattens these rich concepts. By returning to the etymological roots—day as fire, night as measure, dream as joy and deception, comfort as strengthening, sorrow as care—we recover a more nuanced relationship with daily existence.
