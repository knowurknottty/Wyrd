#### Pleasure

**Word:** Pleasure  
**Type:** Noun, Verb (Sensation/Experience)  
**Language:** English  
**Pronunciation:** /ˈpleʒər/

---

**Step 1: Surface Reading**

- **Common meaning:** Feeling of happy satisfaction; enjoyment; sensual gratification; to please
- **Associations:** Happiness, delight, indulgence, luxury, "guilty pleasure," fun
- **Emotional response:** Joy, desire, guilt ("too much pleasure"), satisfaction, craving

The word carries tension between virtue and vice—pleasure as legitimate enjoyment vs. "guilty pleasure," hedonism vs. moderation. Modern usage often commodifies pleasure ("retail therapy") while also suspecting it ("no pain, no gain"). The etymology reveals: PLEASURE comes from BEING PLEASED/AGREEABLE—it is fundamentally relational, about approval and agreement.

---

**Step 2: Root Excavation**

**English → c. 1300:** *plesen*
- Verb: "to please or satisfy (a deity), propitiate, appease"
- From Old French *plaisir*

**Old French → Latin:** *placere*
- Meaning: "to be acceptable, be liked, be approved"
- Related to *placare* "to soothe, quiet, calm"
- From PIE *pl(e)hk- "to agree, be pleasant"

**Latin → PIE:** *pl(e)hk-
- Meaning: "to agree, be pleasant"
- Cognates in Tocharian *plak-* "to agree, permission"

**The Noun Development:**
- Late 14c. *plesance* "enjoyment, delight"
- From Old French *plaisance*
- From *plaisant* "pleasing"
- Eventually "pleasure"

**Critical Discovery:** PLEASURE comes from **AGREEABLENESS/APPROVAL**—*placere* meant being acceptable, being approved of. Pleasure is fundamentally relational: it requires someone/something pleased.

**The Please/Placate Connection:**
- *Placere* = be pleasing
- *Placare* = to soothe, placate
- Same root
- Pleasure and peace connected

**The Propitiation Origin:**
Early "please" (c. 1300):
- "Please or satisfy a deity"
- Propitiate the gods
- Make oneself acceptable
- Religious approval

Shift to:
- Human pleasure
- Secular enjoyment
- Individual sensation
- Lost the relational aspect

**Step 3: Semantic Shift**

| Period | Meaning | Evidence |
|--------|---------|----------|
| PIE | Agree, be pleasant | *pl(e)hk-* |
| Latin | Be acceptable, approved | *Placere* |
| c. 1300 | Please deity, propitiate | Religious usage |
| Mid-14c. | Satisfy a person | "Be agreeable to" |
| Late 14c. | Delight, attract | "Excite agreeable sensations" |
| c. 1500 | "Be pleased" (inverted) | Scottish origin |
| 1620s | "Please do this" (imperative) | Shortened from "if it please you" |
| Late 14c. | Pleasure (noun) | "Enjoyment, delight" |

**What was added:**
- Individual sensation emphasis
- "Pleasure principle" (psychology)
- "Pleasure center" (neuroscience)
- Hedonism/consumerism
- "Guilty pleasure" concept

**What was lost:**
- Relational aspect (pleasing others)
- Religious propitiation
- Approval/agreement sense
- Connection to placation

**Who benefited:**
- Consumer economy (selling pleasure)
- Entertainment industry
- Food industry ("pleasure foods")
- Psychology (pleasure principle)

**The "Complacent" Connection:**

- "Very pleased"

- Lost "please others," kept "pleased with self"

**Step 5: The Liberation Angle**

**The Relational Paradox**

Pleasure = *placere* = "be acceptable, approved":
- Originally relational
- Someone must be pleased
- Approval required
- Social, not solo

Modern individualization:
- "My pleasure"
- Personal sensation
- Brain chemistry
- Lost the "to whom?"

But the etymology suggests:
- Pleasure needs witness
- Approval matters
- It's about fit, agreement
- "Please" still requires object

**The Propitiation Echo**

Early "please" = satisfy deity:
- Divine approval
- Cosmic harmony
- Spiritual pleasure
- Sacred dimension

Secularized:
- "Pleasure" = mere sensation
- Lost transcendence
- Consumer pleasure
- Empty calories

What if pleasure:
- Still required witness?
- Had sacred dimension?
- Was about fitting in?
- Not just consumption?

**The Peace/Pleasure Connection**

*Placere* / *placare*:
- Pleasure / placate
- Same root
- To please = to pacify
- Pleasure brings peace

Modern opposition:
- Pleasure vs. peace (hedonism vs. asceticism)
- But etymologically united
- True pleasure pacifies
- Peace is pleasant

**The Hidden Assumption**

Pleasure assumes:
- Sensation is good
- Satisfaction is desirable
- There's someone/something to please
- Approval feels good

But pleasure also:
- Can be empty
- May require another's exploitation
- Can be addiction
- "Guilty" may be appropriate
