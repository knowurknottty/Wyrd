#### Science

**Word:** Science  
**Type:** Noun (System)  
**Language:** English  
**Pronunciation:** /ˈsaɪəns/

**Step 1: Surface Reading**
- Common meaning: Systematic study of the natural world through observation and experiment
- Associations: Facts, objectivity, proof, laboratories, authority, progress
- Emotional response: Respect, intimidation, skepticism, hope

**Step 2: Root Excavation**
- Middle English: *science* (knowledge, learning)
- Old French: *science* (knowledge, learnedness)
- Latin: *scientia* (knowledge, expertise, comprehension)
- Latin: *scire* (to know, to understand)
- PIE: *skey-* (to cut, to split, to separate)

**The Semantic Shift:**
- Original: To cut/separate → to distinguish → to know
- Shift: The process of knowing → the institutionalized method
- Added: Exclusive claim to legitimate knowledge
- Lost: The cutting aspect—knowledge as separation

**Step 3: Related Forms**
- Conscience (knowing together—*con* + *scire*)
- Conscious (aware, knowing)
- Schism (a split)
- Schist (rock that splits easily)
- Scissors (cutting tool)
- Scythe (cutting tool)

**Step 4: The Liberation Angle**

Science comes from *scire*—to know—which comes from *skey-*—to cut. To know is to cut. Knowledge is separation.

This is profound. Science cuts. It separates subject from object. Observer from observed. Variable from control. It splits the world into analyzable pieces. This is its power—and its limitation.

The etymology suggests: Science cannot know wholeness because its method is cutting. It knows parts superbly. But the cuts themselves—the separations—are artifacts of the method, not nature.

When science says "the self is an illusion" or "free will doesn't exist," remember: it speaks from the cutting. It has separated the subject from itself and found... nothing. Because the cut has already divided what was whole.

**Reclaiming Strategy:**
Use science for what it does well: knowing parts. Do not ask it about wholes, meanings, values, or experience. These require different ways of knowing—integration rather than separation.

Be conscious of *con-science*—knowing-together. This is different from science (*scientia*). One cuts apart. One holds together.

When someone says "science proves," ask: "What did it have to cut to prove this?" Sometimes the cut matters. Sometimes what was excluded was the important part.
