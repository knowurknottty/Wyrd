#### Moon

**Word:** Moon  
**Type:** Noun (Celestial Body/Deity)  
**Language:** English  
**Pronunciation:** /muːn/

---

**Step 1: Surface Reading**

- **Common meaning:** Earth's natural satellite; source of reflected light at night; cycles through phases monthly
- **Associations:** Night, cycles, femininity, madness (lunacy), mystery, romance, tides, measurement
- **Emotional response:** Wonder, mystery, fear, romance, connection to cycles

---

**Step 2: Root Excavation**

**The Chain of Descent:**

| Stage | Form | Meaning | Notes |
|-------|------|---------|-------|
| **Modern English** | *moon* | Earth's satellite; month | Dual meaning |
| **Middle English** | *mone* | The moon | From Old English |
| **Old English** | *mona* | The moon (masculine noun) | Grammatically male |
| **Proto-Germanic** | *menon-* | The moon | Common Germanic |
| **PIE** | *me(n)ses-* | Moon, month | From root *me-* (2) |
| **PIE** | *me-* (2) | To measure | The measurer of time |

**The Critical Etymological Evidence:**

> "Middle English *mone*, from Old English *mona*, from Proto-Germanic *\*menon-*... from PIE *\*me(n)ses-* 'moon, month,' from root *\*me-* (2) 'to measure' in reference to the moon's phases as an ancient and universal measure of time." — *Online Etymology Dictionary*

The moon is **the measurer**. The PIE root *me-* (2) means "to measure," and the moon (*men-*) is named for its function as the **original clock and calendar**. The moon's phases provided the first reliable measure of time longer than a day but shorter than a year.

This explains the double meaning: **moon = month**. The word for the celestial body is also the word for the time period it measures. This is true across Indo-European:
- **Latin:** *mensis* "month" (from same root)
- **Greek:** *men* "month," *mene* "moon"
- **Sanskrit:** *masah* "moon, month"

The Old English moon was **masculine** — grammatically "he" rather than "she." This differs from the sun (feminine) and creates an intriguing gender reversal from modern expectations.

**The PIE *me-* (2) Descendants:**
- **Sanskrit:** *masah* "moon, month"
- **Greek:** *mene* "moon," *men* "month"
- **Latin:** *mensis* "month" → **menstrual** (monthly cycle)
- **English:** **moon**, **month**, **Monday**

**Step 3: Semantic Shift**

**From Measurer to Mystery: The Moon's Semantic Journey**

| Era | Meaning | Context |
|-----|---------|---------|
| **PIE** | The measurer, time-keeper | Functional naming |
| **Proto-Germanic** | *menon-* — moon/month | Dual meaning established |
| **Old English** | *mona* — masculine measurer | Grammatically male |
| **Modern English** | Moon as romantic/mysterious | Cultural associations |
| **1665** | Extended to other planets' satellites | Scientific expansion |

**What Was Lost:**

The original meaning of moon as **measurer** is largely forgotten. We think of the moon as mysterious, romantic, feminine, but rarely as the **original clock** that enabled agriculture, navigation, and calendar-keeping.

The PIE root *me-* (2) "to measure" reveals the **instrumental function** of the moon. Before artificial timepieces, the moon was how humans measured durations longer than a day:
- **Month:** one lunar cycle
- **Year:** 12 lunar cycles (with adjustment)
- **Tides:** governed by lunar gravity

The moon was not merely observed; it was **used**. It was a tool, a technology, a necessity for civilization.

**The Tungl Alternative:**

Old Norse had another word for moon: **tungl**, "heavenly body." This was "replacing *mani* in prose" and is cognate with:
- **Gothic:** *tuggl* "heavenly body"
- **Old English:** *tungol* "heavenly body, constellation"

This suggests an older Germanic layer of astronomical vocabulary, later displaced by the PIE *men-* form.
