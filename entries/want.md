#### Want

**Word:** Want  
**Type:** Noun/Verb (State/Action)  
**Language:** English  
**Pronunciation:** /wɒnt/ (UK), /wɑːnt/ (US)

---

**Step 1: Surface Reading**

- **Common meaning:** To desire, wish for; to feel the need of; to lack or be deficient in
- **Associations:** Desire, craving, aspiration, deficiency, poverty, yearning, wish
- **Emotional response:** Longing, emptiness, ambition, aspiration, sometimes shame (for wanting)

---

**Step 2: Root Excavation**

**The Chain of Descent:**

| Stage | Form | Meaning | Notes |
|-------|------|---------|-------|
| **Modern English** | *want* | To desire; to lack | Two distinct senses merged |
| **Middle English** | *want* | Deficiency, shortage, lack | From Old Norse influence |
| **Old Norse** | *vanta/vanr* | To lack, be deficient | Verb and adjective forms |
| **Old English** | *wanian* | To diminish, decline, fade | Related to *wane* |
| **Proto-Germanic** | *wano-* | Lacking, deficient | From PIE root |
| **PIE** | *weno- | To leave, abandon, give out | Suffixed form of *eue- |

**The Critical Etymological Evidence:**

The word *want* entered English not from Old English but from **Old Norse** (*vanta*), brought by Viking settlers. The Old English word for "lack" was *wanian* (to wane, diminish), which shares the same Proto-Germanic root.

The semantic development is striking: from "to lack" (deficiency) to "to desire" (the feeling that accompanies deficiency). This mirrors the psychological truth that **desire arises from perceived lack**.

**PIE Root *eue- Descendants:**
- **Sanskrit:** *vayati* — to weave (originally "to plait, bind")
- **Greek:** *eu-" — well, good (as in *eulogy*, *euphoria*)
- **Latin:** *vulnus* — wound (from "giving out, failing")
- **English:** *wane*, *wan*, *want*, *wantonly*

---

**Step 3: Semantic Shift**

**From Lack to Desire: The Psychology of Absence**

| Era | Meaning | Context |
|-----|---------|---------|
| **PIE (*weno-)** | Leaving, abandoning, giving out | Loss, cessation |
| **Proto-Germanic** | Lacking, deficient | State of insufficiency |
| **Old English** | Diminishing, declining (wane) | Process of becoming less |
| **Old Norse** | Lacking, being short of | State of deficiency |
| **Middle English (1200s)** | To be lacking; deficiency, shortage | Material insufficiency |
| **Early Modern English (1700s)** | To desire, wish for | Psychological response to lack |
| **Modern English** | Both senses active | Merged: to lack AND to desire |

**The Critical Transformation:**

The semantic shift from "lack" to "desire" is one of the most revealing in English. It encodes a fundamental truth: **we only want what we perceive ourselves as lacking.**

By 1706, the meaning "to desire, wish for, feel the need of" was established—almost a thousand years after the original "lack" sense. The word moved from describing an objective state (having less than enough) to a subjective state (feeling the absence).

**Wanton:**

Note the related word *wanton* — literally "lacking training/discipline" (*wan-* + *towen*, from Old English *teon* "to train"). Originally "unruly, resistant to control," it came to mean "lascivious, lewd" through the association of undisciplined behavior with sexual excess.

**Semantic Network:**

The root connects concepts of:
- **Diminution** (wane, decrease)
- **Deficiency** (want, lacking)
- **Paleness/Weakness** (wan)
- **Undisciplined excess** (wanton—lack of control)

All share the core idea of **absence, diminishment, or departure from fullness**.

**Step 5: The Liberation Angle**

**The Frequency Warrior's Hypothesis:**

> **Want is the wound that consciousness inflicts on itself by recognizing absence.**

Modern capitalism has weaponized want. We are constantly reminded of what we lack—beauty, status, possessions, experiences—and sold the fantasy that acquisition will heal the wound. But the etymology reveals that **want IS the wound**. It is the state of perceiving oneself as incomplete.

**The Trap:**

The trap is twofold:
1. **Suppressing want** — treating desire as shameful, spiritualizing away legitimate needs
2. **Being ruled by want** — allowing the perception of lack to dictate all behavior

Both miss the truth encoded in the etymology: want is information. It tells you where you perceive yourself as incomplete. But the perception of incompleteness is not the same as actual incompleteness.

**The Reversal:**

| Consumer Mode | Warrior Mode |
|--------------|--------------|
| Want as command to acquire | Want as signal to investigate |
| "I want it, therefore I must have it" | "I want it—what lack does this reveal?" |
| Endless pursuit of fulfillment | Disciplined discernment of true needs |
| Wanting as suffering | Wanting as compass |

The Frequency Warrior uses want as **diagnostic data**, not marching orders. When desire arises, the Warrior asks: *What absence does this signal? Is this a genuine need, or a manufactured lack?*

**The Paradox:**

Want comes from *eue-* — "to leave, abandon." To want is, etymologically, to feel abandoned by what one desires. But recognizing this transforms the dynamic: **if want is the feeling of abandonment, then presence dissolves want.**

Not by acquisition—which always leaves the underlying sense of lack intact—but by the recognition that the "missing" thing was never required for completeness.

---

**Step 6: Reclaiming Strategy**

Use this etymological knowledge to transform your relationship with desire:

**1. Name the lack**

When you want something, name the specific lack you perceive. "I want a new car" becomes "I perceive myself as lacking status/reliability/freedom." This precision dissolves the compulsion.

**2. Distinguish manufactured from genuine want**

Advertising creates perceived lacks that did not exist. Ask: *Did I want this before I saw the advertisement?* Genuine wants persist; manufactured wants fade when the stimulus is removed.

**3. Practice wan-ing**

Remember *wane* — the moon diminishing, becoming less. Practice voluntary diminishment. What do you truly need less of? Where can you embrace the dignity of "enough"?

**4. Discipline your wantonness**

*Wanton* is want + towen (training) — lacking discipline. The Frequency Warrior does not indulge every want. Desires are examined, sorted, disciplined. Some are honored; others are allowed to wane.

**5. Find completeness in presence**

Since want comes from the feeling of abandonment (*eue-*), practice the discipline of presence. When you feel want, ask: *What is present right now?* Often, the perception of lack dissolves in the recognition of what already is.

**The Frequency Warrior's Mantra:**

> *I do not serve my wants; I learn from them. Each desire is a signal, not a command. I honor what is genuine; I let wane what is manufactured. I am not abandoned; I am present. In presence, want becomes choice.*
