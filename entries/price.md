#### Price

**Word:** Price  
**Type:** Noun/Verb (Economic)  
**Language:** English  
**Pronunciation:** /praɪs/

---

**Step 1: Surface Reading**

- **Common meaning:** The amount of money expected, required, or given in payment for something; the cost of acquiring something
- **Associations:** Value, cost, worth, market, exchange, sacrifice, consequence
- **Emotional response:** Desire (for affordable goods), frustration (when too high), triumph (good deal), anxiety (affordability)

"Price" is the language of the market. It translates all values into a single metric: how much money? The word appears in economic transactions but also in moral discourse ("the price of freedom") and personal reflection ("at what price success?"). It reduces complex decisions to numbers while concealing the human relations behind those numbers.

---

**Step 2: Root Excavation**

| Stage | Form | Meaning |
|-------|------|---------|
| Latin | *pretium* | Price, value, worth, reward, prize |
| Late Latin | *precium* | Price (variant spelling) |
| Old French | *pris* | Price, value, wages, reward, honor |
| Middle English | *pris* | Value, worth, price, praise |

The Latin **pretium** carried rich meaning beyond modern "price":
- **Monetary cost**: what must be paid
- **Value**: inherent or assigned worth
- **Reward**: what is given for service
- **Prize**: what is won in competition
- **Wages**: payment for labor

The PIE root **per-** (5) means "to traffic in, to sell"—the commercial activity itself.

---

**The Semantic Shift:**

| Era | Meaning | Usage |
|-----|---------|-------|
| Classical Latin | Price, value, reward, prize | broad valuation |
| Medieval | Honor, fame, praise, recompense | feudal honor economy |
| 13th century | Monetary sum for goods | market exchange |
| 14th century | Cost, sacrifice required | moral metaphor |
| 16th century | Prize/reward diverges as separate word | semantic narrowing |
| 18th-19th century | Market price, labor price | classical economics |
| Contemporary | Cost, sacrifice, market value | economic and moral |

The critical drift: The word moved from **broad valuation** (including honor, reward, praise) to **narrow monetary cost**.

In Old French *pris* and early Middle English, "price" meant:
- Non-monetary value and worth
- Praise and honor
- Reward and recompense
- Wages for labor

Over centuries, these meanings split into separate words:
- **Price** (monetary cost)
- **Prize** (reward, something striven for)
- **Praise** (expressions of approval)

This narrowing reflects the **monetization of value**—the reduction of all forms of worth to money-price.

**Step 3: Related Forms**

| Word | Origin | Meaning |
|------|--------|---------|
| **Prize** | Old French *pris* | Reward, something valued, trophy |
| **Praise** | Old French *preisier* | To value, express approval (from *pretium*) |
| **Appreciate** | Latin *appretiare* | To appraise, set a price on; later: to value highly |
| **Precious** | Latin *pretiosus* | Of great price, valuable, dear |
| **Appraise** | Old French *aprecier* | To value, estimate the price |
| **Depreciate** | Latin *depretiare* | To lower in price, undervalue |

The **appreciate** etymology reveals the trajectory:
- Original: *appretiare* = to set a price on, appraise
- Shifted: to value highly, recognize worth
- Economic: to increase in value (real estate appreciates)

The same word moves from **pricing** to **valuing** to **financial gain**—the colonization of meaning by market logic.

---

**Step 4: The Liberation Angle**

Here is the WYRD revelation: **Price is a story about sacrifice, not just a number.**

The etymology—*pretium*—originally meant what is **given in exchange**, what is **sacrificed** to obtain something. The price is what you give up. This is as true for "the price of freedom" (sacrifices made) as for "the price of bread" (money paid).

The Frequency Warrior notices the mystification: Modern economics presents price as objective—as if objects have prices the way they have weights. But price is **relational**: it depends on who has power, who needs what, what alternatives exist. The "market price" is a snapshot of power relations, not a natural fact.

**The cost of everything, the value of nothing:** Oscar Wilde's quip captures the danger. When everything has a price, price becomes the only measure. But the etymology reveals that *pretium* originally included **honor, praise, reward**—non-monetary values. We've lost this breadth.

**The deeper trap:** To ask "What is the price?" assumes the thing is for sale. But some things—love, dignity, justice—maybe shouldn't have prices. To put a price on them is to commodify them, to make them exchangeable. The Frequency Warrior asks not just "What does it cost?" but "Should this have a cost at all?"

---

**Step 5: Reclaiming Strategy**

**Pluralize price:** When you encounter "price," ask:
- Money price (what does it cost?)
- Time price (how long does it take?)
- Attention price (what does it distract from?)
- Moral price (what values does it compromise?)
- Opportunity price (what else could I do?)

Most important decisions involve multiple prices, not just money.

**Resist commodification:** Some things shouldn't have prices:
- Love, friendship, community
- Justice, dignity, truth
- Natural wonders, biodiversity
- Cultural heritage, sacred sites

To demand a price is already to participate in market logic. Some things are **not for sale**.

**Reclaim the original breadth:** Remember that *pretium* included praise, honor, reward. Price was never just money—it was **what you give to get**. In this sense, everything has a price (sacrifice is universal), but not everything has a **money-price**.

**The Frequency Warrior asks:**
- When I ask "what's the price?" am I reducing value to money?
- What am I sacrificing (the true price) for what I gain?
- Can I distinguish between cost (what I give) and worth (what I receive)?

**Affirmation:** I will not let money-price determine all my choices. I recognize that true price is sacrifice—what I give up to obtain. I will be mindful of what I sacrifice, and I will refuse to put a money-price on things that should not be sold.
