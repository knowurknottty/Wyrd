#### Wake

**Word:** Wake  
**Type:** Verb, Noun (Action/State)  
**Language:** English  
**Pronunciation:** /weɪk/

---

**Step 1: Surface Reading**

- **Common meaning:** To cease sleeping; to become alert; vigil before burial; track left by ship
- **Associations:** Alarm clocks, coffee, morning grogginess, funeral gatherings, awareness
- **Emotional response:** Resistance ("don't want to wake"), relief ("glad to wake"), grief ("wake for the dead"), vigilance

The word carries tension between voluntary and involuntary consciousness—being forced awake versus waking naturally. Modern usage often treats waking as interruption (alarms) while also seeking "wakefulness" as mindfulness ideal. The etymology reveals: WAKE comes from BEING LIVELY/VIGOROUS—it's about vigor and strength, not just consciousness.

---

**Step 2: Root Excavation**

**English → Middle English:** *waken*, *wakien*
- From Old English *wacan* (intransitive) and *wacian* (transitive)
- Meaning: "to wake up, arise, be awake, watch"

**Old English → Proto-Germanic:** *wakēną* / *wakjaną*
- From PIE *wog-ē-* (suffixed o-grade form)
- Root: *weg-* "to be strong, lively"

**Proto-Germanic → PIE:** *weg-*
- Meaning: "to be strong, be lively, be awake"
- Also source of:
  - **Watch** (being alert)
  - **Vigil** (Latin *vigil* from same root)
  - **Vigor** (from Latin *vigere*)
  - **Vegetable** (that which grows, is lively)
  - **Velocity** (swiftness, liveliness)

**Critical Discovery:** WAKE comes from **STRENGTH/VIGOR**—not just "not sleeping" but "being lively, strong, alert." The same root gives us VIGIL, VIGOR, WATCH.

**The Three Wake Verbs:**
OE had three related verbs:
1. *Wacan* - to wake up (intransitive)
2. *Wacian* - to be awake (stative)
3. *Wæccan* - to wake someone (transitive, source of "watch")

**The Ship's Wake:**
- Different origin! From Middle Low German *wake*
- Meaning "hole in ice" or "track"
- Related to Old Norse *vök* "hole in ice"
- Unrelated to waking—pure coincidence of form

**The Funeral Wake:**
- From vigil tradition
- Watching over the dead
- Staying awake in honor/grief
- Related to *vigil* (same root)

**Step 3: Semantic Shift**

| Period | Meaning | Evidence |
|--------|---------|----------|
| PIE | Be strong, lively | *weg-* |
| Proto-Germanic | Wake, be alert | *wakēną* |
| Old English | Wake up, watch | *wacan*, *wacian*, *wæccan* |
| Late 14c. | "Wake" (vigil for dead) | Funeral tradition |
| c. 1500 | "Waken" (causative) | Transitive usage |
| Mid-15c. | Ship's wake (different origin) | Track in water |
| 1898 | "Wake up" (figurative) | "How long I slept I cannot tell, but woke at length" |

**What was added:**
- Alarm clock association
- "Wake up" as metaphor ("wake up to reality")
- Ship's wake (different word!)
- Caffeine culture
- Mindfulness "wakefulness"

**What was lost:**
- Vigorous/lively connotation
- Connection to strength
- Natural waking (replaced by alarms)
- Vigil tradition (mostly)

**Who benefited:**
- Coffee industry
- Alarm clock manufacturers
- Meditation teachers ("wakefulness")
- Funeral industry (wake tradition)

**Step 4: Related Forms**

**From PIE *weg-* "be strong, lively":**

**Germanic branch:**
- **Wake** - to be alert
- **Waken** - to cause to wake
- **Awake** - from OE *on wacan*
- **Watch** - from OE *wæccan*
- **Bivouac** - from German *Beiwache* "extra watch"

**Latin branch (vig-):**
- **Vigil** - staying awake
- **Vigor** - strength, liveliness
- **Vigilant** - watchful
- **Invigorate** - give strength
- **Vegerable** (old) → **Vegetable** - growing thing

**Extended derivatives:**
- **Velocity** - from Latin *velocitas* (swiftness)
- **Reveille** - from French *réveiller* (to wake)

**English compounds:**
- **Wake-up call** - literal and figurative
- **Wakefulness** - state of being awake
- **Waking hours** - time spent awake
- **Wakeboard** - using ship's wake
- **Wake-up** - an awakening
- **Wide awake** - fully alert
- **Wake the dead** - very loud
- **In the wake of** - following (from ship's wake)

**The "Awake" Connection:**
- **A-wake** = on/up + wake
- Originally *on wacan*
- The prefix intensifies
- "Fully wake"

**Step 5: The Liberation Angle**

**The Vigor Paradox**

Wake = strength, liveliness:
- Not just "not sleeping"
- But "being vigorous"
- Alertness as strength
- Consciousness as power

Modern sleep culture:
- Waking = suffering (alarms)
- "I need coffee to wake up"
- Wakefulness as obligation
- We've lost the vigor

But the etymology suggests:
- Waking should be vigorous
- Natural waking is powerful
- Alertness is strength
- "I wake" = "I am strong"

**The Three Verbs Lost**

OE had nuance:
- *Wacan* = happen to wake
- *Wacian* = be in waking state
- *Wæccan* = cause to wake

Modern "wake" collapses these:
- No distinction
- No grammatical subtlety
- Loss of linguistic richness

**The Funeral Wake**
Staying awake for the dead:
- Vigil (same root as vigor)
- Not sleeping = honor
- Wakefulness as sacrifice
- Strength shown through non-sleep

Modern:
- "Wake" is social gathering
- Often not actually awake
- Lost the vigil meaning
- Lost the connection

**The Hidden Assumption**

Wake assumes:
- Sleep is default
- Waking requires cause
- Alertness is better than rest
- Consciousness is good

But wake also:
- Can be forced, violent
- May come too soon
- Is sometimes unwanted
- Exhaustion is real
