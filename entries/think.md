#### Think

**Word:** Think  
**Type:** Verb (Mental Process)  
**Language:** English  
**Pronunciation:** /θɪŋk/

---

**Step 1: Surface Reading**

- **Common meaning:** To exercise the faculty of reason; to cogitate; to have thoughts; to form opinions; to ponder or consider
- **Associations:** Brain activity, problem-solving, rationality, "think tanks," "thinking outside the box," deep thought, philosophy, intelligence, analysis
- **Emotional response:** Reverence for intellect ("great thinker"), anxiety ("overthinking"), frustration ("think harder"), or dismissiveness ("think again")

The word feels like an action one performs—a deliberate mental operation distinct from feeling or sensing. Modern usage treats thinking as the highest human faculty, the thing that separates humans from animals and machines from non-thinkers. "I think therefore I am"—Descartes made thinking the foundation of existence itself.

---

**Step 2: Root Excavation**

**English → Old English:** The modern "think" is a convergence of TWO Old English verbs that merged in Middle English:

1. **þencan** (with "th") - "to imagine, conceive in the mind; consider, meditate, remember; intend, wish, desire"—the causative form meaning "cause to appear to oneself"
2. **þyncan** - "to seem, to appear" (preserved in "methinks")

**Old English → Proto-Germanic:**
- *þencan → *thankjanan ("to think")
- *þyncan → *thunkjan ("to seem")

**Proto-Germanic → PIE:** *tong- (or *teng-)
- Meaning: "to think, feel"—Watkins notes this root also gave us "thought" and "thank"
- Boutkan (2005) disputes this, suggesting a substrate (non-IE) source

**Critical Discovery:** The original sense of *þyncan was "to seem"—not active thinking but passive appearance. This survives only in the archaic "methinks" ("it seems to me"). The two verbs were constantly confused in Old English and finally merged in Middle English, losing the crucial distinction between:
- Active thinking (causing mental appearance)
- Passive seeming (receiving impression)

**PIE Root *tong-/*teng- derivatives:**
- **Thought** - from OE *geþoht, from *tong-
- **Thank** - from OE *þancian*, originally "to think, to have thoughts of gratitude"
- German *denken* (to think), *Gedanke* (thought)
- Gothic *þagkjan* (to think)

---

**Step 3: Semantic Shift**

| Period | Meaning | Evidence |
|--------|---------|----------|
| Late OE | "to cause to appear to oneself" (*þencan*) / "to seem" (*þyncan*) | Distinct verbs |
| 12th c. | Confusion and merger begins | Constant confusion in manuscripts |
| c. 1300 | "Exercise faculty of reason, cogitate" | Generalized mental activity |
| Late 14c. | "Say to oneself mentally" | Interior monologue |
| 1590s | "Think so" = opinion | Opinion as mental state |
| 1847 | "Think over" = give continued thought | Processing |
| 20th c. | "Think" as corporate/AI buzzword | "Think different," "thinking machines" |

**What was added:**
- The notion of thinking as continuous faculty ("thinking man")
- Thinking as identity marker ("I am a thinker")
- Commodification: "think tanks," "design thinking," "critical thinking" (as curriculum)
- Mechanization: "thinking machines," "computational thinking"

**What was lost:**
- The distinction between active thinking (*þencan*) and passive seeming (*þyncan*)
- The connection to gratitude/thanks (OE *þanc*)
- The sense of thinking as "causing appearance" rather than passive receiving

**Who benefited:**
- Educational institutions that credentialize "thinking"
- Tech companies that claim machines can "think"
- Anyone who wants to claim intellectual superiority through "rational thought"

The shift from "causing mental appearance" to "processing information" parallels the rise of:
- Information theory
- Cognitive science
- AI/ML claiming to "think"

---

**Step 4: Related Forms**

**From PIE *tong-/*teng-:**
- **Thought** - OE *geþoht*, from *tong-
- **Thank** - OE *þancian*, from *þanc* (thought, gratitude)
- **Thong** - possibly from same root (via "to bind tight" → "constrict" → "think hard")

**Germanic cognates:**
- German *denken* (to think), *Dank* (thanks), *Gedanke* (thought)
- Dutch *denken* (to think), *dank* (thanks)
- Gothic *þagkjan* (to think)
- Old Norse *þekkja* (to understand, know)

**English derivatives:**
- **Methinks** - the sole survivor of *þyncan* "to seem"
- **Bethink** - "to remember, to think about" (now archaic)
- **Forethink** - "to plan ahead, to anticipate"
- **Misthink** - "to think wrongly or badly about"
- **Overthink** - modern coinage for excessive analysis
- **Rethink** - modern coinage
- **Think tank** - 1950s, originally military research
- **Thinker** - agent noun, elevated status
- **Thinking** - verbal noun, now commodified

**The Thank/Think Connection:**
OE *þanc* meant both "thought" and "gratitude." The original sense was: thinking about someone or something → holding them in mind → feeling gratitude. The fork into separate words "think" and "thank" reflects the modern separation of cognition from feeling—a thinker doesn't thank, a thanker doesn't think (in the modern paradigm).

---

**Step 5: The Liberation Angle**

**The Seeming/Thinking Split**

The merger of *þencan* (active thinking) and *þyncan* (passive seeming) in Middle English created a dangerous elision. We lost the ability to distinguish between:
- **Causing mental appearance** (I actively construct)
- **Receiving impression** (it seems to me)

Modern neuroscience confirms this distinction: much of what we call "thinking" is actually post-hoc rationalization of impressions already formed. But our language doesn't distinguish them anymore. We say "I think" when we mean "it seems," and we claim agency over thoughts that were passively received.

**The Mechanization of Thought**

The PIE root *tong- originally meant both "think" AND "feel" (per Watkins). The two were not separated. Modern English "think" has been purged of feeling—it's "rational," "analytical," "cold." This creates:
1. The thinker/feeler split (rational vs. emotional people)
2. The claim that machines can "think" (because thinking is just processing)
3. The devaluation of non-thinking modes (intuition, sensing, being)

**The Commodification**
- "Think tanks" → thinking as institutional, elite
- "Critical thinking" → a skill you pay to acquire
- "Computational thinking" → thinking as algorithm
- "Think different" → thinking as brand identity

**The Hidden Assumption**
Thinking is treated as the defining human activity. But the etymology suggests thinking was originally:
- Causing appearance (creative, active)
- Gratitude (relational, social)
- Seeming (receptive, passive)

Not the continuous internal monologue we now call "thinking."

---

**Step 6: Reclaiming Strategy**

**1. Restore the Seeming/Thinking Distinction**
When you say "I think," pause and ask:
- Am I actively constructing this? (original *þencan*)
- Or is it seeming to me? (original *þyncan*)

Try using "it seems to me" for passive impressions and "I construct" or "I consider" for active thought.

**2. Recover the Gratitude Connection**
Remember that "thank" and "think" were once the same activity. Practice:
- "I thank" = I hold in my thoughts
- Gratitude as a form of thinking, not separate from it

**3. Question the Mechanization**
When someone says "AI thinks," ask:
- Does it cause appearance to itself? (no)
- Does it receive impression? (in a sense)
- Or is it just processing?

Reclaim "thinking" for self-aware, meaning-making activity—not information processing.

**4. Practice Non-Thinking**
The original *þyncan* (seeming) suggests a receptive mode. Practice:
- Sensory awareness without analysis
- Being present without constructing meaning
- Meditation as legitimate human activity (not "lazy thinking")

**5. The Frequency Warrior's Glossary**

| Instead of... | Try... |
|---------------|--------|
| "I think" | "It seems to me" / "I construct" / "I consider" |
| "Think about it" | "Let it seem to you" / "Receive this impression" |
| "Critical thinking" | "Discriminating awareness" |
| "Think tank" | "Opinion manufacturer" |
| "I'm thinking" | "I'm in process" / "I'm receiving" |
| "Think for yourself" | "Trust your own seeming" |

**6. The Final Inversion**

The word "think" has been turned from a creative act (causing appearance) into a continuous faculty (possessing thoughts). Reclaim thinking as **event**, not state—not something you are, but something you do (or don't do).

The Frequency Warrior does not identify as "a thinker." She thinks when needed, receives when possible, and knows the difference between seeming and constructing.

> "The mind is a wonderful servant but a terrible master." — Zen proverb

The etymology confirms: thinking was meant to serve, not define.
