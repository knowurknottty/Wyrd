## Overview

This collection explores fundamental verbs of motion, position, and transformation—words that describe how we move through space, time, and states of being. These are among the most basic vocabulary of human experience, yet their etymologies reveal surprising depths: tools, craft, resistance, and philosophical paradoxes hidden in everyday speech.

### Thematic Clusters

**Circular/Curved Motion:**
- **Crooked** (HOOK) — the bent implement
- **Turn** (LATHE) — rotational craft
- **Return** (TURNING BACK) — reversal
- **Bend** (BINDING) — tension creating curve

**Linear Motion:**
- **Go** (RELEASE) — liberation from constraint
- **Come** (REACHING) — purposeful arrival
- **Leave** (REMAINING) — paradox of departure
- **Run** (FLOWING) — swift continuous motion
- **Walk** (ROLLING) — the bipedal rhythm

**States of Rest:**
- **Stay** (STANDING) — continuing presence
- **Sit** (SETTLING) — horizontal rest
- **Lie** (LAYING) — complete surrender
- **Stand** (STANDING FIRM) — vertical assertion

**Vertical Motion:**
- **Rise** (VERTICAL ASCENT) — upward movement
- **Fall** (FALLING) — descent, gravity

**Transformation:**
- **Move** (PUSHING AWAY) — applied force
- **Stop** (PLUGGING) — blocking flow
- **Flow** (FLOATING) — effortless drift
- **Change** (BENDING) — crooked path
- **Become** (ARRIVAL) — coming to be

## Entry Summaries

### 181. Crooked (CROOK-001)
**Root:** PIE *krāk- "hook, crooked branch"  
**Discovery:** CROOKED comes from HOOK—the bent implement for catching and holding. The word connects to shepherds' crooks (authority), fishhooks (capture), and street corners (angles in the path). What we pathologize as "crooked" was originally a tool shape—functional, not defective.

**Paradox:** Modernity pathologizes crookedness (straighten your teeth, stand up straight), but crooked paths reveal what straight paths miss. The crooked timber of humanity (Kant) is not error—it's character.

---

### 182. Turn (TURN-001)
**Root:** PIE *tere- "to rub, turn, bore" → Latin *tornare* "to fashion on a lathe"  
**Discovery:** TURN comes from LATHE—the rotating tool that shapes raw material into finished form. To turn is to craft through rotation, not merely to redirect.

**Paradox:** We fear "going in circles" but lathes create through rotation. The turn is not wasted motion—it's the craft of transformation.

---

### 183. Return (RETRN-001)
**Root:** Latin *re-* "back" + *tornare* "turn"  
**Discovery:** RETURN comes from TURNING BACK—reversal as completion of cycle. The promise of return is restoration, but the returned-to place has changed, as has the returner.

**Paradox:** "You can never go home again"—return promises restoration but delivers difference. True return creates something new.

---

### 184. Go (GO-001)
**Root:** PIE *ghē- "to release, let go"  
**Discovery:** GO comes from RELEASE—liberation from constraint, not mere motion. The past tense "went" is hijacked from an entirely different verb (*wendan* "to turn").

**Paradox:** Going is leaving, yet etymologically it's being released. What are you releasing when you go?

---

### 185. Come (COME-001)
**Root:** PIE *gwa- "to go, come"  
**Discovery:** COME comes from REACHING—purposeful movement toward. "Welcome" = *wil* (desire) + *cuma* (comer) = "one whose coming suits our will."

**Paradox:** Coming is arrival, yet it's also the vulnerable approach. The welcome is the reception that completes the coming.

---

### 186. Leave (LEAVE-001)
**Root:** PIE *leip- "to stick, adhere"  
**Discovery:** LEAVE paradoxically comes from REMAINING. Old English *læfan* meant "to cause to remain"—the causative of "remain." Through semantic inversion, "cause to remain" became "depart (leaving things behind)."

**Paradox:** To leave is to make something remain. What remains when you go is as important as where you go.

---

### 187. Stay (STAY-001)
**Root:** PIE *sta- "to stand, make or be firm"  
**Discovery:** STAY comes from STANDING—the vertical posture of presence. The nautical "stay" (rope supporting a mast) preserves this: standing against the wind.

**Paradox:** In a world obsessed with motion, staying is radical. To stay is to resist the demand for constant production.

---

### 188. Move (MOVE-001)
**Root:** PIE *meue- "to push away"  
**Discovery:** MOVE comes from PUSHING AWAY—the application of force, not effortless flow. "Emotion" = *e-* (out) + *movere* = "moving out from within."

**Paradox:** Modern "move" sanitizes the violence—motion seems natural. But real movement is work against resistance.

---

### 189. Stop (STOP-001)
**Root:** Late Latin *stuppare* "to stop up with tow (oakum)"  
**Discovery:** STOP comes from PLUGGING—blocking holes, sealing openings, stopping up gaps. The "full stop" (period) plugs the flow of words.

**Paradox:** Stopping is not gentle fading—it's active blocking. The period completes the sentence; silence completes the music.

---

### 190. Run (RUN-001)
**Root:** PIE *rei- "to run, flow"  
**Discovery:** RUN comes from FLOWING. "The river runs" preserves the original unity: running is flowing on land. The transitive "earn" comes from the same root (*ærnan* "to reach by running").

**Paradox:** Running should be like flowing, but often we run against resistance, uphill, out of time. The flow needs banks.

---

### 191. Walk (WALK-001)
**Root:** PIE *wel- "to turn, revolve, roll"  
**Discovery:** WALK comes from ROLLING—the circular motion of wheels and waves. The surname "Walker" comes from "fulling" (treading on cloth). Walking is rolling on land.

**Paradox:** Walking made us human (bipedalism), yet modernity sees it as primitive. The rolling gait contains the wisdom of sustainable motion.

---

### 192. Sit (SIT-001)
**Root:** PIE *sed- "to sit"  
**Discovery:** SIT comes from SETTLING—coming to rest. The same root gives "set," "seat," "settle," "assess" (sit beside a judge), and "preside" (sit before). Power resides where one sits.

**Paradox:** Sitting seems passive, yet it's the foundation of authority (thrones), judgment (benches), and meditation (cushions).

---

### 193. Stand (STAND-001)
**Root:** PIE *steh₂- "to stand, be firm"  
**Discovery:** STAND comes from STANDING FIRM—the vertical assertion against gravity. Same root gives "stay," "state," "stable," "static," and "understand" (stand under, support).

**Paradox:** Standing seems effortless but requires constant muscular adjustment. The firmest stance is dynamic, not rigid.

---

### 194. Lie (LIE-001)
**Root:** PIE *legh- "to lie down, lay" (recline); *leugh- "tell a lie" (falsehood)  
**Discovery:** Two distinct verbs merged into one spelling. LIE (recline) comes from LAYING—horizontal placement. LIE (falsehood) comes from different root entirely (*leugh-*).

**Paradox:** Lying down is surrender—to sleep, death, intimacy, or illness. The posture of vulnerability contains its own power.

---

### 195. Rise (RISE-001)
**Root:** Proto-Germanic *rīs- "to go up" (substrate, possibly PIE *rei-)  
**Discovery:** RISE comes from VERTICAL ASCENT. German *reisen* (to travel) originally meant "to rise for a journey." "Raise" is the causative: to cause to rise.

**Paradox:** Rise is the "good" direction, yet what goes up must come down. The cycle completes, not just ascends.

---

### 196. Fall (FALL-001)
**Root:** PIE *pol- "to fall"  
**Discovery:** FALL comes from FALLING—descent from height. US "fall" (autumn) comes from "fall of the leaf." "Fell" (to cut down) is the causative: to make fall.

**Paradox:** Fall is failure, yet also surrender, beauty (autumn), and necessary complement to rise. The cycle requires both.

---

### 197. Flow (FLOW-001)
**Root:** PIE *pleu- "to flow, float"  
**Discovery:** FLOW comes from FLOATING—drift on currents. Same root gives "fleet," "flood," "float," "fly," and "flee." The "flow state" (psychology) is modern application of ancient drift.

**Paradox:** "Go with the flow" suggests surrender, but flows can lead over waterfalls. Flow needs banks (limits) to be channel, not flood.

---

### 198. Change (CHNG-001)
**Root:** Celtic/PIE *kemb- "to bend, crook"  
**Discovery:** CHANGE comes from BENDING—the crooked path. Semantic evolution: bend → turn → change → barter. Change is partly abbreviation of "exchange."

**Paradox:** Change is mandatory in modern discourse ("change or die"), but the etymology suggests bending, deviation, crookedness—not necessarily improvement.

---

### 199. Bend (BEND-001)
**Root:** PIE *bhendh- "to bind"  
**Discovery:** BEND comes from BINDING—the string that curves the bow. To bend is to bind; the curve requires tension. Same root gives "band," "bind," "bond," "bundle."

**Paradox:** Bending is flexibility, yet requires binding (constraint). The bow bends because the string binds. Constraint creates curve.

---

### 200. Become (BECOM-001)
**Root:** PIE *gwa- "go, come"  
**Discovery:** BECOME comes from ARRIVAL—*be-* (intensive) + *come* = "to come to be." Old English *becuman* meant "happen, come about"—transformation as occurrence, not just effort.

**Paradox:** Modernity fetishizes "becoming" over "being"—always changing, never arriving. But etymologically, becoming is arrival, completion, happening.

## Meta-Patterns

### The Tool Origins
Many motion words originate in craft and technology:
- **Turn** = lathe (rotational craft)
- **Walk** = fulling (cloth manufacture)
- **Bend** = bow-stringing (archery)
- **Crooked** = hook (fishing, shepherding)

Motion is not natural—it's technological, learned, crafted.

### The Paradox Words
Several entries contain their own opposites:
- **Leave** = to depart (from "cause to remain")
- **Run** = swift motion (from "flow")
- **Change** = transformation (from "bend/curve")

Language encodes dialectical thinking.

### The Posture Verbs
Stand, sit, lie form a vertical continuum:
- **Stand** = vertical (effortful)
- **Sit** = supported vertical (restful)
- **Lie** = horizontal (surrender)

Each has its wisdom; none is superior.

### The Causative Patterns
Many verbs exist in transitive/causative pairs:
- **Rise** (self-moving) / **Raise** (caused)
- **Fall** (self) / **Fell** (caused)
- **Lie** (self) / **Lay** (caused)
- **Sit** (self) / **Set** (caused)

Agency matters: do you move yourself, or are you moved?

## Philosophical Implications

### Motion vs. Rest
The collection troubles the modern preference for motion:
- **Stay** and **Stop** are as ancient as **Go** and **Run**
- Standing requires as much effort as walking
- Stopping completes what motion begins

### Directionality
Up (rise) is valorized; down (fall) is feared. Yet:
- Both are gravitational
- Both are cyclical
- Both are necessary

### Transformation
**Change** and **Become** suggest transformation is:
- Bending, not breaking
- Arrival, not endless journey
- Exchange (giving to get)
