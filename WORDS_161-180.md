## Summary Overview

This batch explores the etymological foundations of human spatial orientation — how we conceptualize and name distance (far, close, distant), elevation (high, low, up, down, above, below, top, bottom), centrality (center, middle, edge, side), directionality (front, back), and lateral orientation (left, right, straight). These words reveal how embodied experience shapes abstract thought, and how prehistoric technologies (the hooked peg, the compass point, the stretched cord) continue to structure our most basic spatial vocabulary.

## Cross-Cutting Themes

### 1. The Body as Spatial Template

Many spatial terms derive from embodied experience:
- **Front** (*frons*) = forehead, the expressive face
- **Back** (*bakam*) = broad rear surface
- **Top** (*toppa-*) = tuft of hair, the visible crest
- **Bottom** (*fundus*) = foundation, what supports the body
- **Left** (*lyft*) = the weaker hand
- **Right** (*riht*) = the straight/direct hand

**Implication:** Spatial abstraction begins in bodily orientation. We don't just have bodies; we think *through* them.

### 2. The Technology of Position

Several words derive from primitive technologies:
- **Close** (*klau-*) = the hooked peg used as bar or bolt
- **Center** (*kentron*) = the sharp point, goad for cattle
- **Edge** (*ak-*) = the sharpened blade
- **Straight** (*streht*) = the stretched cord

**Implication:** Our most "natural" spatial concepts are mediated by ancient tools. Position is technology.

### 3. The Paradox of Up/Under

PIE *upo* gives us:
- **Up** = up from under
- **Hypo-** = under (hypothesis, hypodermic)
- **Over** = above (intensive of *upo*)
- **Sub** = under (Latin)

**Implication:** Elevation and depth are not opposites but phases of a cycle. What is up emerges from what is under.

### 4. The Moralization of Direction

- **Right** = straight = just = lawful
- **Left** = weak = awkward = sinister
- **High** = exalted = superior
- **Low** = humble = inferior

**Implication:** Spatial orientation becomes moral hierarchy. The Frequency Warrior must recognize these associations as cultural, not natural.

### 5. Germanic Specificity

Some words are uniquely Germanic, revealing a distinct spatial worldview:
- **Down** (*of dune*) = off the hill (English-only)
- **Back** (*bakam*) = broad rear surface (Germanic-only)

**Implication:** Not all spatial concepts are universal. Language shapes spatial experience.
